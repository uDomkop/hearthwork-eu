# Gate Hestia Emergency Border Notification and Council Coordination Instrument

## Preamble

The Adriatic Surge has exposed a critical gap in the Gate Hestia's crisis procedures. The crisis is real. Arrivals have exceeded ITA's reception capacity. Secondary movement has created cascading pressures. One Member State (FRA) responded with unilateral internal border controls, which the ILCB correctly ruled ultra vires — the Charter's Assembly-Locked architecture does not permit Gate Hestia instruments to authorise the suspension or restriction of internal free movement.

However, the ILCB's ruling revealed something equally important: the Charter lacks a *procedural* mechanism for rapid notification, Council review, and binding coordination when a Member State or Companion faces a genuine border emergency. FRA's border closure, while ultra vires as an authorisation, signalled a real institutional need.

FRA therefore proposes a **notification and coordination instrument** — not an authorisation instrument. This proposal does not ask the Gate Hestia to authorise unilateral border controls. It establishes a mandatory notification obligation, a fast-track Council review procedure, and a binding coordination directive authority that enables the Council to respond to declared emergencies within 48 hours. Any measure taken by a state without prior coordination directive is subject to automatic legal sunset.

This proposal respects the Assembly-Locked Domain: it does not authorise any restriction of free movement. It creates procedural obligations and Council coordination authority — both squarely within Gate competence.

---

## Article 1 — Emergency Border Situation: Declaration and Notification

### 1.1 Definition of Emergency Border Situation

An **Emergency Border Situation** exists when a Member State or Companion notifies the Gate Hestia Council that it faces, in any single rolling 30-day period, a sudden and substantial surge in undocumented arrivals or external border pressures that:

(a) Exceed historical average arrivals by 150% or more;
(b) Threaten the integrity of external border management operations or reception capacity at external Schengen borders;
(c) Create cascading secondary movement pressures affecting one or more other Gate members.

The notifying state shall present documented evidence of the surge (arrival records, transit data, reception service stress indicators). The Gate Hestia Council shall determine by simple majority whether the declared situation meets the definition.

### 1.2 Mandatory Notification Obligation

Any Member State or Companion experiencing, or reasonably foreseeing, an Emergency Border Situation shall notify the Gate Hestia Council **within 24 hours** of the situation arising. Notification shall include:

(a) Description of the surge (origin, volumes, demographics, transportation mode);
(b) Current capacity status (reception, processing, detention, border infrastructure);
(c) Proposed response measures, if any;
(d) Request for Gate Council coordination, if needed.

Failure to notify within 24 hours shall not invalidate a subsequent notification, but may be noted in Council deliberations as affecting the urgency assessment.

### 1.3 Council Convening Obligation

Upon receipt of notification under 1.2, the Gate Hestia Council **shall** convene within **48 hours**. The Council may convene via written procedure (expedited consultation with vote within 72 hours) or in-person session at the request of any Member State or the Chair.

## Article 2 — Gate Council Coordination Review and Directive

### 2.1 Council Assessment

Within the 48-hour convening window, the Gate Hestia Council shall:

(a) Verify whether the notified situation meets the Emergency Border Situation definition;
(b) Review the notifying state's proposed measures;
(c) Assess whether any proposed measure affects the free movement rights of other Gate members or Companions;
(d) Determine whether a Coordination Directive is warranted.

### 2.2 Coordination Directive Authority

If the Council determines that an Emergency Border Situation exists, the Council may issue a **Coordination Directive** that:

(a) Authorises the notifying state to implement specific, time-limited border management measures;
(b) Specifies the measures authorised, their territorial scope, and their duration (not to exceed 90 days without renewal);
(c) Imposes conditions on implementation, including:
   - (i) Non-discrimination in application;
   - (ii) Documentation and reporting to the Council every 14 days;
   - (iii) Consultation with affected Gate members before implementing any measure affecting free movement or transit routes;
   - (iv) Exemption or reduced procedures for Union citizens and Schengen visa holders;

(d) Requires the Council to review the measure within 45 days and determine whether continuation is warranted.

### 2.3 Companion Safeguard — Mandatory Consultation

Any Coordination Directive that would authorise a measure affecting a Companion state's Schengen border crossing, entry procedures, or transit rights shall **require the affected Companion's consent** before issuance.

In the case of any measure potentially affecting Schengen-wide free movement or transit infrastructure, the Council shall:

(a) Notify all Companion states of the measure, the notifying state's request, and the Council's preliminary assessment;
(b) Grant Companion states **5 business days** to register a formal objection or propose amendments;
(c) Incorporate Companion feedback in the final Coordination Directive, or record any Companion's formal Suspensive Veto notice (if applicable under Charter VII.5).

### 2.4 Measure Content Constraints

No Coordination Directive shall:

(a) Authorise the suspension, restriction, or limitation of free movement rights for Union citizens or valid Schengen visa holders based on nationality, ethnicity, origin, or religious affiliation;
(b) Authorise measures that discriminate between Gate members or Companions;
(c) Authorise measures that would require unilateral modification of EU-third-country agreements without Assembly involvement;
(d) Authorise detention, reception, or processing arrangements that violate non-refoulement or international humanitarian standards (Bench Hestia standards apply).

---

## Article 3 — Unilateral Measures: Automatic Sunset and Gate Council Authority

### 3.1 Unilateral Emergency Measures Without Prior Coordination Directive

Where a Member State or Companion implements a border management measure in response to a declared Emergency Border Situation **without prior Council authorisation through a Coordination Directive**, that measure shall:

(a) Be legally permissible under this instrument;
(b) Automatically expire after **14 days** from implementation, unless:
   - (i) The notifying state has notified the Council and requested a Coordination Directive under Article 1.2–2.2; AND
   - (ii) The Council has issued a Coordination Directive authorising that specific measure.

### 3.2 Notification and Council Action

Any state implementing an emergency measure under 3.1 shall notify the Council **within 24 hours** of implementation (or within 24 hours of becoming aware that the measure will be taken). The Council shall then conduct the review process under Article 2.1–2.3.

If the Council determines that the measure was warranted and meets Coordination Directive criteria, the Council may issue a prospective Coordination Directive authorising continuation of the measure from the date of the Directive forward. No Coordination Directive may validate, cure, or extend the legal effect of any action taken prior to the Directive's issuance. If the Council does not issue a Coordination Directive within 14 days, the measure expires automatically.

### 3.3 Legal Status of Expired Measures

Any measure that expires under 3.1 shall be deemed legally void ab initio with respect to any actions taken after the 14-day period. Actions taken during the 14-day period remain valid but are subject to post-hoc Council assessment of proportionality and necessity.

---

## Article 4 — Coordination Directive Issuance and Voting

### 4.1 Voting Requirement

A Coordination Directive shall be issued by the Gate Hestia Council by **simple majority** (3 states, any population distribution). The 2-state, 126.5M threshold does not apply — coordination is procedural, not normative.

### 4.2 Companion Co-Sponsorship and Support

This proposal anticipates co-sponsorship by Poland (POL), signalling to the Council that the eastern land border context is integral to the rationale for the coordination instrument. Any Coordination Directive issued under this Article shall remain binding on all Gate members and Companions, regardless of how they voted.

---

## Article 5 — Relationship to Schengen, Free Movement, and the Assembly-Locked Domain

### 5.1 Non-Authorisation Character

This instrument does **not** authorise any Gate Hestia member or the Gate Hestia Council to suspend, restrict, limit, or condition free movement rights for Union citizens under the Schengen framework.

Any measure that would require such a suspension or limitation must be justified through Assembly procedures and must comply with Assembly-Locked provisions in the Charter (I.10). A Coordination Directive under this instrument cannot override Assembly-Locked rights.

### 5.2 Procedural, Not Normative

A Coordination Directive is a procedural instrument: it coordinates the timing, notification, and Council review of emergency measures. It is not a normative instrument that authorises permanent restrictions, modifies the scope of Gate Hestia member obligations, or alters rights defined in the Bench Hestia.

### 5.3 Free Movement Preservation

All Coordination Directives shall explicitly preserve the rights of Union citizens and valid Schengen visa holders to move freely within the territories of Gate members and Companions, subject only to the specific procedural restrictions authorised in the Directive (e.g., temporary identity document verification, health screening, security risk assessment) and only where those restrictions are:

(a) Non-discriminatory;
(b) Proportionate to the declared Emergency Border Situation;
(c) Specified with measurable criteria (e.g., "all persons" or "persons from specific third countries") not based on protected characteristics.

---

## Article 6 — Bench Hestia Interaction and Non-Refoulement

### 6.1 Asylum and Reception Rights

All persons subject to any emergency border measure authorised under this instrument remain subject to the Bench Hestia's asylum procedure harmonisation framework and international non-refoulement obligations.

Any state implementing a border measure shall:

(a) Screen arrivals for asylum claims and manifestly well-founded humanitarian protection requests;
(b) Refer asylum claimants to the Bench Hestia procedure;
(c) Permit access to legal representation;
(d) Comply with Bench Hestia standards on detention, reception, and processing.

### 6.2 Bench Hestia Review

Any state or Companion may invoke the Bench Hestia's dispute resolution procedures to challenge whether a border measure authorised by Coordination Directive complies with Bench standards. The Bench Hestia has authority to rule on the measure's consistency with asylum and humanitarian law, without authority to nullify the Coordination Directive itself — but with authority to order specific implementation modifications.

---

## Article 7 — Duration, Renewal, and Sunset

### 7.1 Coordination Directive Duration

A Coordination Directive shall specify the measure's duration, not to exceed **90 days** from issuance without renewal.

### 7.2 Renewal

The notifying state may request Council renewal of a Coordination Directive if the Emergency Border Situation persists. The Council shall conduct a new assessment under Article 2.1 and may renew the Directive for an additional 90 days. Total duration shall not exceed 270 days (three renewal cycles) without material change in circumstances.

### 7.3 Automatic Expiry

Any Coordination Directive that is not renewed shall expire automatically at the end of its specified duration. Any measure taken under an expired Coordination Directive is subject to Article 3.3 sunset and expiry rules.

---

## Article 8 — Council Reporting and Transparency

### 8.1 Notification Register

The Gate Hestia Council shall maintain a public register of:

(a) All Emergency Border Situation notifications received;
(b) All Coordination Directives issued, their scope, duration, and renewal status;
(c) Council assessment outcomes.

### 8.2 Reporting to Assembly and Bench

The Council shall report quarterly to the Assembly on all Emergency Border Situations and Coordination Directives issued in the prior quarter. The Bench Hestia shall receive a copy of each Coordination Directive for review of consistency with Bench standards.

### 8.3 Audit and Review

The Gate Hestia's independent auditor (appointed under Article 2.4 of the DEU Solidarity Regulation 001) shall audit implementation of all border measures authorised under Coordination Directives, with particular attention to:

(a) Discriminatory application;
(b) Adherence to specified duration and territorial scope;
(c) Compliance with non-refoulement and Bench standards;
(d) Proportionality assessment.

---

## Article 9 — Entry into Force and Relationship to Existing Law

### 9.1 Entry into Force

This instrument enters into force upon Council approval and publication in the Gate Hestia Register.

### 9.2 Relationship to Article 1 of the DEU Solidarity Regulation (001)

This instrument operates in parallel to the binding relocation mechanism in DEU's 001. The relocation mechanism addresses *systematic* crisis response via automatic population-based sharing. This instrument addresses *emergency* crisis response via rapid notification and Council coordination.

A state may invoke both instruments simultaneously: a notification under this Article 1.2 may trigger both a relocation obligation (if the threshold under 001 Article 1.1 is met) and a Coordination Directive (if an Emergency Border Situation is declared under this Article 1.1).

### 9.3 Relationship to FRA's Prior Ultra Vires Border Closure

FRA's unilateral reimposition of internal Schengen border controls in response to the Adriatic Surge was ruled ultra vires by the ILCB. This instrument does not retroactively validate that action. However, FRA's action was motivated by institutional gap that this instrument fills. FRA will regularise its border position through future notification and coordination procedures under this instrument, if Gate members vote to enact it.

## Notes for ILCB

### Classification Questions

FRA anticipates the ILCB will consider:

1. **Domain:** Is the notification and coordination procedure a Gate Hestia matter? *FRA's position:* Yes — border emergency procedure, notification obligations, and Council coordination authority are squarely within Gate competence. The instrument does not authorise any substantive restriction of free movement; it is procedural.

2. **Assembly-Locked Compliance:** Does this instrument violate the Assembly-Locked Domain (I.10)? *FRA's position:* No — the instrument explicitly does not authorise any Gate Hestia member to suspend or restrict free movement. Any Coordination Directive is constrained by Article 5.3 to preserve Union citizen and valid Schengen visa holder movement rights. Measures that would require Assembly-Locked suspension must go through Assembly procedures.

3. **Companion Inclusion:** Can Companion states (particularly NOR) be bound by a notification obligation and subject to Coordination Directive authority? *FRA's position:* Yes — Companions participate in Gate with full decision-making authority and Schengen rights. Notification and coordination obligations are reciprocal. However, Article 2.3 provides Companions with mandatory consultation rights if a measure affects their interests, consistent with Companion governance principles.

4. **External Reach:** Does this instrument constitute "external reach" requiring Assembly ratification? *FRA's position:* No — the instrument deals with internal Gate notification, Council review, and border measure coordination. It does not alter free movement rules, create external obligations, or ratify third-country agreements. Border measures authorised under Coordination Directives may affect third-country nationals, but that is inherent to border management and does not trigger External Reach.

### Anticipated Objections and FRA's Responses

- **NOR may worry:** Does Article 2.3's "mandatory consultation" language actually protect Companion interests? *Response:* Yes — any measure affecting a Companion's borders or free movement requires affirmative Companion consent before Directive issuance. If a Companion registers a Suspensive Veto under Charter VII.5, that veto is recorded and narrated. This preserves NOR's veto right.

- **DEU may ask:** Is this instrument redundant with the relocation mechanism in 001? *Response:* No — relocation is systemic (triggers automatically at population threshold); coordination is procedural (triggers at emergency notification). A state may face an emergency without meeting the relocation threshold.

- **ITA may ask:** Does this enable states to close borders to asylum claimants without Council approval? *Response:* No — Article 4.2 limits Coordination Directives to measures that do not suspend free movement. Asylum claimants are subject to Bench procedures. The Council cannot authorise discrimination.

- **POL may note:** Does this replace the relocation mechanism with a discretionary coordination scheme? *Response:* No — POL's objection to 001 is noted. This instrument is independent and does not modify 001's binding character.

### FRA's Transparency on Intent

This proposal is FRA's resubmission of 002-B, reframed. FRA is **not** asking for authorisation to close borders unilaterally. FRA is asking for:

- A procedural obligation to notify the Council within 24 hours when an emergency exists;
- A Council obligation to convene within 48 hours;
- Council authority to issue a *coordination directive* (not an authorisation) that regularises the notification procedure and gives the Council a fast-track response mechanism;
- An automatic sunset on any measure taken without Council coordination within 14 days.

FRA is **not** prepared to negotiate:

- The **24-hour notification window** (FRA considers 72 hours, as in the original 002-B, too long for genuine emergencies);
- The **Council's 48-hour convening obligation** (FRA considers this rapid response essential);
- The **Assembly-Locked preservation** in Article 5.1 (FRA accepts the ILCB's ruling that free movement is Assembly-Locked);
- The **Companion safeguard** in Article 2.3 (FRA considers this fair given NOR's Veto notice).

FRA **is** prepared to negotiate:

- The 14-day automatic sunset duration (could be adjusted to 21 or 28 days);
- The renewal cycle (could be lengthened to 180 days per cycle);
- The Council voting requirement (currently simple majority; could be qualified majority).

## Co-Sponsorship

**Poland (POL)** is anticipated as co-sponsor. POL's eastern land border context mirrors ITA's Mediterranean pressure and FRA's internal dynamics. POL faces uncontrolled transit from external borders and has committed to seeking a Gate coordination mechanism. This proposal serves POL's interest in rapid Council response to border surge scenarios without accepting mandatory relocation (which POL opposes in 001).

FRA and POL will jointly signal that this instrument addresses the *procedural gap* that 001 left unresolved.

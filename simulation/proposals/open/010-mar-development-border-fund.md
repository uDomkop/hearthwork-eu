# MAR-Gate Hestia Development and Border Cooperation Fund

## Preamble

The Adriatic Surge cannot be resolved through Gate Hestia internal relocation mechanisms alone. The root of the crisis is structural: irregular migration from North Africa to the Mediterranean corridor continues because economic and security conditions in MAR and MAR's Sahel neighbours drive emigration as a survival strategy. Without addressing those structural drivers, and without formalising MAR's cooperation as a Charter instrument, the relocation mechanism will be forced into permanent operation, exhausting frontline states and creating cascading instability.

MAR is a sovereign partner of the Gate Hestia, not a buffer state. MAR controls a major transit corridor and has demonstrably reduced arrival volumes through cooperative transit management since the crisis began. However, MAR's cooperation cannot be contingent on ad hoc bilateral deals or unilateral threats of withdrawal by individual Member States. MAR requires a formal, multilateral instrument that:

1. Recognises MAR as a Charter partner with binding capacity-building commitments from Gate Hestia members;
2. Establishes a joint development fund for border infrastructure, customs capacity, and economic development in emigration-pressure regions of MAR;
3. Frames cooperation as mutual obligation, not as MAR's performance metric or contingent on migration reduction targets;
4. Ensures continuity across electoral cycles and government changes in both MAR and Gate Hestia member states.

This proposal establishes the **MAR-Gate Hestia Development and Border Cooperation Fund**, capitalised at **€120 million over five years** (€24M annually), administered jointly by MAR and the Gate Hestia Council through a Joint Committee with equal representation. The Fund is a Charter instrument, not a bilateral arrangement. All Gate Hestia Member States contribute via Fiscal Key weighting. MAR contributes equivalent in-kind cooperation (personnel, facilities, data sharing, and transit management).

This proposal is submitted by MAR via its Observer Seat standing under VI.10 and is co-sponsored by ITA and DEU, who have signalled support for formalising MAR's role in the Charter framework.

---

## Article 1 — Establishment and Capitalisation

### 1.1 Creation of the Fund

The Gate Hestia Council hereby establishes the **MAR-Gate Hestia Development and Border Cooperation Fund** (the "Fund"), a Charter instrument administered jointly by the Kingdom of Morocco and the Gate Hestia Council through a dedicated Joint Committee (Article 3).

### 1.2 Initial Capitalisation

The Fund shall be capitalised at **€24 million per annum for five years** (total €120 million), indexed annually to inflation (HICP baseline year 2026). Initial capitalisation is triggered upon Council approval and MAR's formal acceptance of the Fund charter.

### 1.3 Fiscal Key Contribution

The Fund shall be financed through mandatory contributions from all Gate Hestia Member States in proportion to their Fiscal Key allocation, calculated as follows:

**Annual Gate Hestia contribution = €24M × (State Fiscal Key share)**

Estimated contributions (based on Regulation 001 Fiscal Key weightings):
- DEU: €9.6M (40% of €24M)
- FRA: €7.68M (32%)
- ITA: €4.8M (20%)
- POL: €1.92M (8%)

No state may refuse payment without triggering the Light-Out Clause (Charter VI.4 ¶3).

### 1.4 MAR In-Kind Contribution

MAR contributes equivalent value through in-kind cooperation, valued at **€24 million per annum**, comprising:

(a) Personnel deployment: Border management, customs, and migration control officials assigned to joint operations and training programmes (€8M valuation per annum);
(b) Facility access: Border infrastructure, office space, secure communication networks, and database access for joint cooperation (€6M valuation per annum);
(c) Data sharing: Real-time provision of transit flow data, human smuggling networks intelligence, and regional security assessments (€6M valuation per annum);
(d) Transit management: Continued operational cooperation on border control, secondary movement prevention, and irregular route interdiction (€4M valuation per annum).

The Joint Committee shall review in-kind contributions annually to ensure MAR's contribution remains equivalent to the Fund capitalisation.

## Article 2 — Fund Eligible Expenditure

### 2.1 Development and Capacity-Building

The Fund may deploy resources for:

(a) **Border Infrastructure Cooperation:**
   - (i) Joint border checkpoint modernisation (biometric systems, secure communications, facility upgrades) at MAR-EU transit borders;
   - (ii) Development of inland transit control points along migration corridors (reducing irregular trans-shipment points);
   - (iii) Logistics and equipment for joint patrols and monitoring operations;

(b) **Customs and Migration Capacity:**
   - (i) Training programmes for MAR customs and migration officials (funded study visits, professional certifications, technical expertise transfer);
   - (ii) Systems development for electronic manifest tracking and travel document verification;
   - (iii) Forensic and document authentication capacity-building;

(c) **Regional Development Investment:**
   - (i) Economic development projects in high-emigration regions of MAR (small business support, vocational training, rural infrastructure);
   - (ii) Youth employment and apprenticeship programmes in emigration-pressure provinces (southern Morocco, Atlantic coast regions);
   - (iii) Social cohesion and community stabilisation programmes in vulnerable populations;

(d) **Migration Management Training:**
   - (i) Joint training for border personnel, police, and intelligence services (human trafficking identification, asylum seeker identification, protection standards);
   - (ii) Legal education programmes on international labour law and migrant worker protections;
   - (iii) Civil society capacity-building on rule-of-law and human rights in migration contexts.

### 2.2 Prohibited Expenditures

The Fund **shall not** finance:

(a) Detention facilities, enforcement-only operations, or measures targeting irregular migrants for apprehension or punishment (except where necessary as part of coordinated inter-agency human trafficking interdiction);
(b) Any activity that criminalises irregular entry or retrospectively penalises MAR nationals or third-country nationals who have used irregular routes;
(c) Forced repatriation programmes or involuntary return operations;
(d) Intelligence gathering or surveillance activities targeting MAR's civilian population or opposition political movements;
(e) Any programme or activity framed as an instrumentality of European migration control rather than as genuine development cooperation.

### 2.3 Conditionality and Partnership Framing

Fund expenditures shall be structured with the explicit recognition that:

(a) MAR's cooperation is a sovereign policy choice, not a contingent obligation dependent on meeting European migration reduction targets;
(b) Fund activities are framed as **capacity-building and economic partnership**, not as performance metrics or conditions of MAR's international standing;
(c) Any capacity-building programme shall include explicit recognition of MAR's ownership and decision-making authority over its own institutional development;
(d) Fund activities shall respect MAR's labour law, constitutional protections, and migration law — no Fund activity may require MAR to adopt legal standards lower than MAR's current law.

## Article 3 — Fund Governance: Joint Committee

### 3.1 Composition and Structure

The Fund shall be governed by a **Joint Committee** with equal representation:

(a) **MAR Delegation:** Four representatives appointed by the Kingdom of Morocco government, including:
   - One representative from the Ministry of Interior (border/migration affairs);
   - One representative from the Ministry of Economy (development affairs);
   - One representative with expertise in civil rights or social development;
   - One representative at ministerial or vice-ministerial level with authority to commit MAR positions;

(b) **Gate Hestia Delegation:** Four representatives appointed by the Gate Hestia Council, including:
   - One representative from the Council Presidency;
   - One representative from a Member State designated by rotation (or by seat-holder alternation);
   - One independent technical expert in border management or development financing;
   - One representative with expertise in human rights and non-refoulement standards (potentially Bench Hestia liaison);

(c) **Secretariat:** A Joint Secretariat, with staff seconded from both MAR and Gate member states, responsible for day-to-day programme administration, accounting, and reporting.

### 3.2 Decision-Making: Equal Voice

(a) All substantive decisions regarding Fund allocation, project approval, and policy direction require **consensus** between MAR and Gate delegations. If consensus cannot be reached after good-faith deliberation (not less than 30 days), either delegation may appeal to the Gate Hestia Council and MAR's Anchor Hestia representatives for binding dispute resolution under Article 3.5.

(b) Routine administrative decisions (staffing, budget execution within approved categories, quarterly reporting) may be delegated to the Secretariat, subject to Secretariat authority limits set by the Joint Committee.

(c) Either delegation may propose agenda items; the Committee shall convene quarterly in ordinary session and may convene extraordinary sessions at either delegation's request.

### 3.3 Fund Administration and Audit

(a) The Fund shall be held in trust by the Gate Hestia Council, with a separate accounting facility and dedicated bank account in a neutral jurisdiction (e.g., SWIFT-registered account in Switzerland or the Netherlands, under Joint Committee authority).

(b) All expenditures exceeding €500,000 shall require written approval from both MAR and Gate delegations.

(c) A **Joint Independent Auditor**, appointed by the Joint Committee for a three-year term, shall:
   - (i) Conduct quarterly audits of Fund accounts;
   - (ii) Verify that all expenditures comply with Article 2 (eligible/prohibited activities);
   - (iii) Publish quarterly public audit reports (with appropriate security redactions for ongoing operations);
   - (iv) Report directly to both the Joint Committee and the Gate Hestia Council.

(d) If the Auditor identifies a violation of Article 2.2 (prohibited expenditures) or a material misuse of Fund resources, the matter shall be immediately escalated to the Gate Hestia Council and to MAR's government. Either party may suspend Fund disbursement pending investigation (not to exceed 60 days without Joint Committee agreement to extend).

### 3.4 Transparency and Public Reporting

(a) The Joint Committee shall publish an annual public report summarizing:
   - Fund capitalisation and expenditure by category;
   - Progress against agreed capacity-building objectives;
   - Outcomes and impact assessments for completed projects;
   - Any disputes or suspensions, with explanation;
   - Auditor findings and remedial actions taken.

(b) Project-level detail may be withheld where disclosure would compromise operational security (border interception tactics, intelligence partnerships) or personal privacy (individual beneficiary information), provided that aggregate project categories remain publicly reported.

(c) All procurement contracts exceeding €250,000 shall be published in summary form (vendor name, project description, amount, timeline) within 30 days of signature.

### 3.5 Dispute Resolution

If MAR and the Gate Hestia Council delegation cannot reach consensus on a Fund allocation decision, the matter shall be referred to:

(a) **First resort:** MAR's representatives in the Anchor Hestia Council and the Gate Hestia Council may jointly convene a dispute panel (one representative from each delegation plus one neutral chair, appointed by the Gate Hestia Chair in consultation with MAR).

(b) **Panel authority:** The panel shall determine whether the disputed allocation complies with Article 2 (eligible/prohibited) and with Article 3.2 (equal voice principle). The panel's decision is binding on both delegations.

(c) **Escalation:** If either party believes the panel's decision contradicts the Charter or exceeds its authority, MAR or the Gate Council may invoke the ILCB under Charter VII.8.

## Article 4 — Duration, Amendment, and Termination

### 4.1 Initial Term

The Fund is established for an initial term of **five years** from entry into force. At the conclusion of Year 4, the Joint Committee shall conduct a comprehensive review and shall recommend whether the Fund should be renewed, expanded, or reformed.

### 4.2 Renewal and Expansion

If the Joint Committee consensus recommends renewal, the Fund shall continue for an additional five-year term at the same annual capitalisation (adjusted for inflation) or at an amended level if both MAR and the Gate Hestia Council agree. Renewal requires explicit Gate Hestia Council vote (same threshold as original approval) and MAR formal acceptance.

### 4.3 Amendment Authority

The Fund charter may be amended by:

(a) Joint Committee proposal approved by both MAR and Gate delegations (consensus);
(b) Submission to the Gate Hestia Council as a regulation amendment proposal;
(c) Approval by the Gate Hestia Council (same voting threshold as the original Fund establishment) and formal acceptance by MAR.

### 4.4 Termination

The Fund may be terminated by either MAR or the Gate Hestia Council only upon:

(a) At least **12 months' written notice**;
(b) Good-faith negotiation between delegations for 6 months to address the reason for termination;
(c) If no resolution is reached, the terminating party may formally notify intention to terminate, effective 6 months later.

Upon termination, any unspent balance shall be:
   - (i) Applied to completion of approved projects (subject to completion deadlines);
   - (ii) Returned to contributing Gate Hestia Member States pro-rata to their contributions, minus any encumbered or committed amounts.

## Article 5 — Relationship to Article 2.5 Cooperation Framework (Regulation 001)

### 5.1 Harmonisation with Third-Country Cooperation

Regulation 001 Article 2.5 establishes a general framework for Gate Hestia third-country cooperation agreements, including capacity-building grants and labour migration pathway development. This Fund operates as a **dedicated, multilateral** implementation instrument for that framework as applied to MAR.

### 5.2 Amendment to Regulation 001 Article 2.5 — MAR Proposed Language

MAR tables a proposed amendment to Article 2.5 of Regulation 001 (DEU's enacted cooperation article), to be adopted separately or bundled with DEU's Proposal 005 amendment:

#### Current Text (Article 2.5)

```
Funds may be transferred to third-country governments or international organisations for border management and legal labour migration pathway development, conditional upon:

(a) A written cooperation agreement lodged with the Gate Hestia Council;
(b) Independent verification of use and results;
(c) Respect for international labour standards and rule-of-law norms, as assessed by the Bench Hestia.
```

#### Proposed Amendment (New Article 2.5, replacing existing text)

```
2.5 Third-Country Cooperation — Partnership Framework

Funds may be transferred to third-country governments or international organisations for border management and legal labour migration pathway development under the following principles:

(a) **Partnership of Equals:** Cooperation with third countries, including Accord States and strategic partners, shall be founded on recognition of the partner state's sovereignty, self-determination, and equality in decision-making. No cooperation agreement shall frame a third-country partner as a "buffer," "transit management tool," or instrumental extension of European border control.

(b) **Binding Capacity-Building Commitments:** Any cooperation agreement shall include explicit, reciprocal capacity-building commitments from Gate Hestia Member States, including technical assistance, training, infrastructure investment, and professional exchange. European states shall commit to building the partner state's institutions on timelines and to standards set jointly by both parties.

(c) **Protection of Partner Sovereignty:** Cooperation agreements shall explicitly recognise the partner state's right to set its own migration and border management policies, independent of European preferences. Gate Hestia contributions shall support the partner state's policy choices, not condition them.

(d) **No Criminalization Provisions:** Cooperation agreements shall not include retroactive or prospective penalties, sanctions, or criminalization against nationals of the partner state or third-country nationals who have used irregular migration routes. Cooperation shall focus on prevention and legal pathways, not enforcement against individuals.

(e) **Labour Rights Protection:** Any cooperation framework addressing labour migration shall include explicit protections for migrant workers' rights, including freedom of association, collective bargaining, fair wage guarantees, and access to judicial remedies for labour violations. Gate Hestia states shall not support cooperation agreements that establish lower labour standards than those applied to EU citizens.

(f) **Verification and Accountability:** Independent verification shall be conducted jointly by the partner state and the Bench Hestia, not unilaterally by Gate Hestia. The partner state shall have standing to challenge verification findings and to request corrections or reapprosal.

(g) **Duration and Contingency:** Cooperation agreements shall be durable and not contingent on migration reduction targets or arbitrary performance metrics. However, agreements may establish benchmarks for institutional development or capacity growth. If compliance failures are identified, remediation timelines shall be established and good-faith assistance provided before any suspension of cooperation.

(h) **Written Agreement and Gate Council Lodgement:** All cooperation agreements shall be in writing, signed by authorised representatives of both parties, and lodged with the Gate Hestia Council. The agreement shall be public (with security redactions if necessary) and available for consultation by all Gate Hestia participants.
```

### 5.3 Application of Amended Language

This Fund shall operate under the amended Article 2.5 language. If the Council approves the amendment, it applies prospectively to all new third-country cooperation agreements. If the Council does not approve the amendment, this Fund remains valid as a standalone multilateral instrument independent of Article 2.5, but MAR signals its expectation that future third-country cooperation agreements honour the partnership principles set out in this amendment proposal.

## Article 6 — Legal Labour Migration Pathways

### 6.1 Relationship to Proposed Regulation 008

The Gate Hestia Council is considering a separate proposal (Regulation 008, co-sponsored by ITA and NOR) establishing binding annual labour migration quotas from MAR to Gate Hestia Member States. This Fund is designed to complement and operationalise that framework by building the institutional capacity in MAR to certify workers, verify credentials, and manage departure procedures that meet EU standards.

### 6.2 Fund Support for Pathway Infrastructure

Eligible Fund expenditure includes capacity-building for:

(a) Labour certification programmes (skills assessment, credential verification);
(b) Language and professional training for prospective migrants;
(c) Consular and administrative procedures for visa issuance and documentation;
(d) Public education and information campaigns in MAR about legal pathways, workers' rights, and protections in EU employment.

### 6.3 No Conditionality on Migration Reduction

The Fund and Regulation 008 (if enacted) shall be framed as **mechanisms for legalising and regularising labour migration**, not as mechanisms to reduce irregular migration through enforcement. Any connection between labour pathways and transit management shall be framed as "expanding legal opportunities reduces irregular demand," not "enforcement + legal pathways = migration control."

## Article 7 — Entry into Force and Initial Implementation

### 7.1 Approval and Ratification

This proposal requires Gate Hestia Council approval by the same voting threshold as other Gate Hestia regulations (double majority: 3 states AND >126.5M). MAR acceptance of the Fund charter is separate and shall be formally notified to the Gate Hestia Council.

### 7.2 Fund Entry into Force

Upon Council approval and MAR acceptance, the Fund enters into force on the date of publication in the Gate Hestia Register. Gate Hestia Member States shall remit their first annual contribution within 45 days of entry into force. The Joint Committee shall convene within 60 days of entry into force for initial governance setup and approval of Year 1 programme allocations.

### 7.3 Year 1 Programme

In Year 1, the Fund shall prioritise:

(a) Establishment of the Joint Committee and Secretariat infrastructure;
(b) Baseline assessment of existing border capacity and development priorities in MAR (jointly commissioned);
(c) Design and initiation of priority border infrastructure and training projects;
(d) Commencement of youth employment and regional economic development pilots in highest-emigration provinces;
(e) Legal pathway infrastructure development to support Regulation 008 (if enacted).

## Notes for ILCB and Gate Hestia Council

### Classification and Competence

**Domain:** Is this Fund a Gate Hestia matter or does it implicate Charter domains beyond Gate?

*MAR's position:* The Fund addresses border management capacity, migration pathway development, and third-country cooperation — all squarely within Gate Hestia competence under VI.2. The development component (economic investment in MAR regions) is not a Gate matter per se, but is a necessary complement to border/migration cooperation and is explicitly tied to capacity-building for those functions. The Fund does not implicate Assembly-Locked free movement (MAR is not a member state) or Bench Hestia asylum procedures (the Fund supports capacity-building, not asylum decision-making). If the ILCB determines that certain Fund activities exceed Gate competence, MAR welcomes a narrow ruling that those activities be restructured as general development assistance outside the Fund, while preserving the Fund's core border management and labour pathway components.

### External Reach and Assembly Ratification

**Issue:** Does this Fund constitute "external reach" requiring Assembly ratification under Charter II.6?

*MAR's position:* No. This Fund does not alter free movement, internal rules, or Treaty relationships. It is a technical cooperation instrument within Gate competence. However, MAR acknowledges that the ILCB may take a different view, and MAR is prepared to accept Assembly ratification if that is the ILCB's ruling, provided that Assembly ratification is advisory and does not grant the Assembly veto power over Joint Committee operations.

### MAR's Observer Seat Standing

**Standing to Propose:** MAR is not a Gate Hestia Member State. Does MAR have standing to propose a regulation via its Observer Seat?

*MAR's position:* Charter VI.10 grants Observer Seats to eligible Accord States and permits observers to "propose amendments." MAR interprets this as authority to propose instruments that directly affect the observer's cooperative relationship with the Hestia. This Fund is precisely such an instrument — it formalises MAR's role in the Charter's border and migration cooperation framework. ITA and DEU co-sponsorship, as full Gate Hestia members, provides formal proposer standing under Charter procedures, while MAR's contribution comes through its Observer Seat intervention. If the ILCB rules that Observer Seats do not permit instrument proposals (only amendments), MAR's proposal should be reclassified as a request for amendment to Regulation 001 Article 2.5, to be formally submitted by ITA or DEU as co-sponsors.

### Relationship to Regulation 008 (Legal Pathways)

This Fund does not depend on Regulation 008's enactment. However, the two instruments are designed as complementary mechanisms. If 008 is enacted, the Fund's labour pathway component becomes a critical implementation tool. If 008 is not enacted, the Fund's labour pathway expenditures remain eligible under Article 2.1(d), supporting voluntary capacity-building independent of a binding legal migration quota.

## Negotiation Flexibility

### What MAR is prepared to negotiate:

- The annual capitalisation level (MAR has proposed €24M; this could be adjusted downward if Gate members face fiscal constraints, or upward if outcomes require expanded investment);
- The in-kind contribution valuation methodology (MAR has provided detailed values; these could be recalibrated by Joint Committee agreement);
- The proportion of Fund resources allocated to development vs. border capacity (MAR has not specified a hard ratio; allocation is flexible subject to consensus);
- The duration of the initial term (MAR proposed 5 years; this could be adjusted with mutual agreement);
- The composition of the Joint Committee (MAR proposed 4+4 parity; this could be adjusted to include additional technical expertise if both parties agree);
- The governance dispute resolution procedure (MAR proposed a three-step escalation; alternative procedures are negotiable).

### What MAR will not negotiate:

- **The binding character of MAR's cooperation.** MAR will not accept framing of its participation as optional, contingent on migration numbers, or subject to unilateral withdrawal by individual Member States. If the Fund exists, it exists as a binding Charter instrument.
- **Partnership parity and sovereignty recognition.** Any final fund charter must include explicit language recognising MAR as a sovereign partner, not as a tool or buffer. The amended Article 2.5 language (or its equivalent) is non-negotiable from MAR's perspective.
- **Prohibition on criminalization and retroactive penalties.** Article 2.2 prohibits any Fund activity that criminalises irregular migrants or retrospectively penalises MAR nationals. This floor is not negotiable.
- **Equal voice in governance.** The Joint Committee must have equal MAR and Gate representation, and decisions must operate on consensus (not Gate-majority override). This is a hard constraint.
- **Labour rights protection.** Any labour migration framework supported by this Fund must include explicit protections for migrant workers' rights, including freedom of association and fair wages. This is non-negotiable.

## Coordination with MAR's Anchor Hestia Position

MAR holds a Council seat in the Anchor Hestia and has abstained on Regulation 003-A (ITA's Mediterranean SAR coordination) as a conditional signal of leverage. MAR signals that this Fund proposal, combined with the ITA/NOR Regulation 008 (legal pathways), addresses the conditions for MAR's conversion from conditional abstention to active cooperation in the Anchor Hestia.

Specifically:

- **If both Proposal 008 (legal pathways) and Proposal 010 (this Fund) are enacted:** MAR will convert its Anchor Hestia participation to active voting support for Mediterranean SAR coordination and related Anchor instruments.
- **If only one of the two is enacted:** MAR will maintain its current Anchor position pending further negotiation.
- **If neither is enacted:** MAR will abstain on all Anchor Hestia matters pending reconsideration of its strategic participation in the Charter framework.

This is a transparent signal of MAR's leverage and its strategic interests, not a threat, but rather a formal statement of the conditions under which MAR's full engagement with the Charter's crisis response mechanisms can be secured.

# Gate Hestia Emergency Solidarity and Border Management Regulation

## Preamble

The Gate Hestia faces a systemic crisis. Frontline Member States, particularly ITA, are experiencing arrival surges beyond their reception and processing capacity. Secondary movement within Schengen has created cascading pressures that now threaten the integrity of free movement itself. One Member State has already reimposed internal border controls unilaterally. This signals the urgent need for binding, permanent solidarity mechanisms — not ad hoc coordination.

DEU proposes a two-pillar regulation: an automatic relocation mechanism triggered by objective thresholds, and a common external border management fund capitalised through the Fiscal Key. Both pillars are necessary. The relocation mechanism ensures that frontline burden is genuinely shared. The border fund enables prevention at source and operational excellence at the external frontier. Together, they restore confidence in the Gate Hestia's fairness and effectiveness.

This proposal respects the Assembly-Locked Domain: it does not suspend or restrict free movement for Union citizens. It establishes mandatory relocation obligations between Member States and Companions, and establishes shared funding for border operations.

---

## Article 1 — Gate Hestia Emergency Solidarity Mechanism

### 1.1 Automatic Relocation Trigger

When any frontline Member State or Companion notifies the Gate Hestia Council that, in any rolling 90-day period, persons subject to asylum or migration procedures have arrived in its territory at a rate exceeding **1,500 per million inhabitants per month** (on a rolling 90-day average), the Council shall immediately declare that state to be in a state of disproportionate reception burden.

The calculation is: (total arrivals in prior 90 days) ÷ (state population in millions) ÷ 3 months = monthly rate per million. When this ratio exceeds 1,500, the trigger is met.

### 1.2 Mandatory Relocation Obligations

Upon the Council's declaration under 1.1, all Member States and Companions in the Gate Hestia are bound by the following relocation obligation:

Each state shall accept and assume full responsibility for persons designated for relocation by the burdened frontline state, calculated as follows:

**Relocation share = (State population ÷ Total Gate population) × (Persons exceeding 1,500/mm threshold)**

No state may declare this obligation "voluntary" or decline it without material consequence to its standing in the Gate Hestia Council and its Fiscal Key allocation (see Article 2).

### 1.3 Persons Subject to Relocation

Persons subject to relocation are those for whom an asylum claim is registered, or who are subject to return procedures, in the territory of the burdened frontline state, and for whom a relocation decision has been made by the frontline state authority designated under Article 1.7.

### 1.4 Relocation Procedures and Rights

All persons relocated under this mechanism remain subject to the Bench Hestia's asylum and judicial cooperation framework. Receiving states shall:

(a) Process asylum claims in accordance with Bench Hestia harmonised procedures;
(b) Provide reception, accommodation, and social support equivalent to standards applied to persons in the receiving state's own asylum system;
(c) Permit family tracing and family unity procedures in accordance with international law;
(d) Grant appeal and administrative review rights equivalent to those available in the frontline state.

### 1.5 Relocation Quotas and Burden Adjustment

Relocation obligations shall be calculated and announced by the Gate Hestia Council within 7 days of the disproportionate burden declaration. The obligation is binding on all Gate participants. However, a state may request a modification to its share based on:

(a) Demonstrated receiving capacity limits (with independent verification);
(b) Pre-existing asylum claims from the same origin in its territory (to avoid concentrating vulnerable populations);
(c) Financial hardship affecting reception services.

The Council shall rule on such requests within 30 days. Any approved modification shall be proportionally redistributed among other states.

### 1.6 Duration and Triggers for Cessation

The relocation obligation remains in force for the 90-day period for which the threshold was exceeded, plus a further 90-day period of scaled-down relocation (50% of calculated share) to enable transition. If a new rolling 90-day period meets the threshold again, the obligation is renewed.

The obligation ceases when the rolling 90-day average drops below 1,200 per million inhabitants per month and remains below that level for a further 60 days.

### 1.7 Implementation Authority and Timeframes

Each frontline state shall designate a relocation authority responsible for selecting persons for relocation. The authority shall consult the receiving state on any person whose health, security, or family circumstances might warrant an alternative receiving state.

Relocated persons shall depart the frontline state within 30 days of relocation decision. The receiving state shall accept arrival within 45 days of decision.

## Article 2 — Gate Hestia External Border Management and Reception Fund

### 2.1 Establishment

The Gate Hestia Council hereby establishes the **Gate Hestia External Border and Reception Fund** (the "Fund"), capitalised through mandatory Fiscal Key contributions and administered by the Council.

### 2.2 Fiscal Capitalisation

The Fund shall be capitalised annually in an amount equal to **€3,000 per person relocated under Article 1, multiplied by the average annual relocation burden forecast by the Council**, plus **€500 million per annum as a floor for operational costs**.

The Fund shall be financed through contributions from Gate Hestia members in proportion to their Fiscal Key allocation. No state may refuse payment without triggering the Light-Out Clause (Charter VI.4 ¶3).

### 2.3 Eligible Expenditure

The Fund may be deployed for:

(a) Reception and accommodation services for relocated persons in receiving states (up to €15,000 per person per annum);
(b) Asylum processing and administrative capacity in frontline states;
(c) Border management operations at the external frontier (personnel, surveillance, inter-agency coordination);
(d) Search and rescue operations in international waters within Gate jurisdiction;
(e) Capacity-building grants to third-country transit partners (including MAR) for transit management, with conditions on labour rights and rule-of-law standards.

### 2.4 Fund Governance and Audit

The Fund shall be administered by the Gate Hestia Council through a dedicated Executive Board, composed of:
- One representative from each Member State and Companion;
- One independent auditor, appointed by the Council for a 3-year term.

The Board shall publish quarterly reports on expenditure and relocation flows. Any proposed expenditure exceeding €50 million in a single state in a single annum shall require Council approval by simple majority.

### 2.5 Third-Country Cooperation

Funds may be transferred to third-country governments or international organisations for border management and legal labour migration pathway development, conditional upon:

(a) A written cooperation agreement lodged with the Gate Hestia Council;
(b) Independent verification of use and results;
(c) Respect for international labour standards and rule-of-law norms, as assessed by the Bench Hestia.

---

## Article 3 — Relationship to Schengen Free Movement

This Regulation does not suspend, restrict, limit, or condition free movement rights for Union citizens under the Schengen framework. The relocation mechanism operates on asylum and migration procedures and persons, not on the movement of citizens.

Unilateral border controls by any Member State or Companion remain subject to the Assembly-Locked Domain rules governing free movement. Any state wishing to justify internal Schengen controls shall do so through Assembly procedures, not Gate Hestia legislation.

---

## Article 4 — Interaction with the Bench Hestia

All persons subject to relocation under Article 1 shall be processed in accordance with the Bench Hestia's asylum procedure harmonisation framework. Where a conflict arises between this Regulation and a Bench Hestia obligation, the Bench Hestia standard shall prevail, except that receiving states shall not be relieved of the relocation obligation itself — only its modalities may be adjusted.

Any third-country cooperation agreement requiring legal rights standards shall be referred to the Bench Hestia for verification before Fund deployment.

---

## Article 5 — Entry into Force and Transition

### 5.1 Entry into Force

This Regulation enters into force upon Council approval and publication in the Gate Hestia Register. It applies immediately to any new disproportionate burden declaration made after entry into force.

### 5.2 Initial Burden Assessment

Within 14 days of entry into force, the Council shall assess whether any current frontline state meets the disproportionate burden threshold. If so, relocation obligations take effect within 7 days of that assessment.

### 5.3 Transition Period

In the first 90 days after entry into force, states may request a 50% reduction in relocation obligations if they can demonstrate that capacity development is in progress. The Council shall consider such requests on a case-by-case basis.

---

## Article 6 — Review and Amendment

The Gate Hestia Council shall review this Regulation after 12 months of operation and shall consider amendments based on:

(a) Experience of relocation implementation;
(b) Changes in arrival flows and frontline pressure;
(c) Feedback from Companions and receiving states.

Any amendment shall require the same voting threshold as the original Regulation.

## Fiscal Implications

### Capitalisation

Year 1 estimated impact: €2.1 billion (assuming 140,000 persons relocated, €15,000 per capita reception + €500M operational floor).

### Fiscal Key Contribution

Contributions by participating state (based on current EU Fiscal Key weightings, adjusted for Companion status):

- DEU: €840M (40% of fund)
- FRA: €680M (32%)
- ITA: €410M (20%, with relief credit for frontline burden)
- POL: €170M (8%)
- NOR: €0M (Companion; may opt in via voluntary contribution)

DEU declares its willingness to pay its full share and to increase it if necessary to enable the mechanism to function effectively.

### External Border Fund

Estimated €500M per annum for operational costs is allocated as:
- 40% (€200M) for frontline state capacity (processing, accommodation)
- 35% (€175M) for external border operations and SAR
- 25% (€125M) for third-country cooperation and legal migration pathways

## Notes for ILCB

### Classification Questions

DEU anticipates the ILCB will consider:

1. **Domain:** Is the relocation mechanism a Gate Hestia matter (border management and migration procedure) or does it implicate Bench Hestia (asylum procedures)? *DEU's position:* The mechanism itself is a Gate matter; the rights of relocated persons are Bench-governed through Article 4. The relocation mechanism does not exceed the Gate Hestia's competence.

2. **External Reach:** Does the third-country cooperation framework (Article 2.5) constitute "external reach" requiring Assembly ratification? *DEU's position:* No — cooperation agreements with third countries are negotiated within Gate competence and do not alter free movement or internal rules. However, DEU is prepared for the ILCB to rule that such agreements require Assembly notice or ratification.

3. **Schengen Integrity:** Does binding relocation between Gate members constitute an interference with Schengen? *DEU's position:* No — relocation is a solidarity obligation between states, not a border control mechanism. It does not close, harden, or condition the free movement of persons.

4. **Free Movement Respect:** Article 3 explicitly preserves Assembly-Locked free movement rights and does not authorise any Gate Hestia suspension thereof.

### Anticipated Objections and DEU's Responses

- **FRA may argue:** The mechanism is too binding; it imposes unilateral obligations on FRA regardless of FRA's preferences. *Response:* That is the purpose of a Member obligation. Optional burden-sharing is not solidarity; it is a pretence. The Charter contemplates binding obligations.

- **POL may argue:** The mechanism is a quota system in disguise; it imposes migration on eastern states against their wishes. *Response:* The trigger is objective (1,500/mm); the relocation obligation is calculated by population, not discretionary allocation. Eastern states participate only when a documented emergency exists. Moreover, POL's concern is acknowledged: Article 1.5 permits modification requests, and Article 2.3(e) provides external border capacity funding that benefits all states, including those less affected by onward movement.

- **NOR may worry:** As a Companion, does this create equal obligation for a non-Member? *Response:* Article 1.2 explicitly includes Companions. NOR has Schengen membership and benefits from the gate framework; obligation is reciprocal. However, Article 2.2 permits Companions to opt into financial contributions rather than mandatory payment.

### Transparency on Intent

This proposal is DEU's opening position. DEU is prepared to negotiate:

- The relocation threshold (currently 1,500/mm) downward if evidence shows a lower rate is still disproportionate;
- The percentage of persons relocated (currently 100% of the threshold excess) downward to, e.g., 80% if burden is genuinely transitional;
- The Fund capitalisation amount (currently €3,000 per person) upward if frontline states require higher support;
- The cessation threshold (currently 1,200/mm) if monitoring shows a different level restores stability.

DEU is **not** prepared to negotiate:

- The **binding character** of the relocation obligation. The word "voluntary" or any equivalent discretionary framing in the final text will trigger a DEU veto;
- Any framework that leaves MAR's cooperation undefined and contingent on bilateral deals;
- Relocation procedures that do not include Bench Hestia due-process rights (Article 4).

# Eastern Border Emergency Infrastructure and Personnel Fund

## Preamble

The Republic of Poland reports heightened irregular movement pressure on its eastern land border, driven by instrumentalised migration through transit countries. This pressure is distinct in nature, geography, and legal context from maritime arrivals in the southern Mediterranean. POL has deployed military personnel to support border guard operations and requires dedicated financial and operational instruments calibrated to land border defence. This proposal establishes a Gate Hestia facility with its own budget line, governance structure, and eligibility criteria, separate from and complementary to any Mediterranean border facility.

## Article 1 — Establishment of the Eastern Border Emergency Instrument (EBEI)

1.1 The Gate Hestia hereby establishes the Eastern Border Emergency Instrument (EBEI), a dedicated financing mechanism for member and companion states managing irregular migration pressure on external land borders.

1.2 EBEI is funded from the Gate Hestia's operational budget under a distinct budget line, independent from any Mediterranean or maritime facility.

1.3 Eligible beneficiaries: Gate Hestia members and companions whose external land borders border territories experiencing documented instrumentalised migration flows, as verified by the Hestia Council upon application.

1.4 For the duration of this instrument (initial term: 36 months from enactment), POL is deemed eligible by operation of this article, without requiring further verification.

## Article 2 — Eligible Expenditure

2.1 EBEI funds may be deployed for:

(a) Physical border infrastructure: fencing, barrier systems, gates, vehicle barriers, surveillance technology, and sensor networks at external land borders;

(b) Personnel and operational costs directly supporting border guard and law enforcement operations: training, equipment, vehicles, communications, processing facilities, and border crossing capacity;

(c) Coordination infrastructure: joint control centres, data-sharing systems, and operational liaison facilities between national border authorities;

(d) Emergency response capacity: rapid deployment reserves, emergency accommodation, and medical facilities at border zones.

2.2 EBEI funds may NOT be deployed for:

(a) Relocation, transfer, or distribution of persons across member or companion territory;

(b) Asylum determination or adjudication processes;

(c) Reception facilities, integration services, or longer-term accommodation for persons after entry into beneficiary state territory;

(d) Third-country development or financial assistance (separate instruments may address this).

## Article 3 — Governance and Allocation

3.1 The EBEI budget is distributed to eligible states by the Hestia Council upon application. Applications shall specify:

(a) The geographic area (border segment or region) for which funds are requested;

(b) The documented migration pressure (border crossings, attempted crossings, transport interdictions) in the preceding 90 days;

(c) The specific projects or operational needs to be funded;

(d) The matching commitment (if any) from the applicant state.

3.2 The Hestia Council shall allocate EBEI resources on a per-application basis, with priority given to borders experiencing documented instrumentalised migration (flows routed or facilitated by third-country actors or criminal networks).

3.3 Fund transfers occur quarterly upon receipt of verified expenditure reports from the beneficiary state.

## Article 4 — Military Personnel Clarification (pending ILCB ruling)

4.1 For clarity, border support operations involving military personnel may be funded under this instrument if the operational objective is border management, movement control, and infrastructure protection, not defence or force projection.

4.2 Costs associated with military personnel deployed in border support roles, including training, equipment, logistics, and salaries attributable to border duty, are eligible for EBEI funding.

4.3 The ILCB is requested to clarify, as a matter of Gate Hestia jurisdiction, whether military personnel deployed in border infrastructure security and irregular movement interdiction fall under Gate Hestia competence or Shield Hestia competence. Pending that ruling, this article permits their funding under EBEI without prejudice.

## Article 5 — Implementation

5.1 This Instrument enters into force on 1 July 2026, or upon enactment by the Hestia Council, whichever is later.

5.2 Initial budget allocation shall be determined by the Hestia Council following double-majority vote on this proposal.

5.3 Member and companion states are not obligated to apply for EBEI funds. Participation is voluntary.

5.4 No state is obligated to contribute beyond its Fiscal Key obligation. Voluntary additional contributions are permitted.

## Fiscal Implications

The EBEI requires a dedicated budget allocation from the Gate Hestia's operational funds. POL does not propose a new total budget for the Gate Hestia, but rather reallocation of existing Gate funds to this new instrument or modest expansion as the Hestia Council determines proportional to the scale of pressure.

Estimated year-one cost: EUR 80–120 million (infrastructure and personnel support for one to three beneficiary states). Financing model: standard Fiscal Key, with voluntary top-ups from participating states encouraged.

All member and companion states pay their Fiscal Key contribution to the Gate Hestia. The EBEI is one of multiple instruments within the Gate budget. No state is required to fund this instrument beyond its standard Fiscal Key payment.

## Notes for ILCB

**Formal ILCB Ruling Request:**

POL respectfully requests that the ILCB issue a preliminary ruling on the following classification question:

*Do costs associated with military personnel deployed in border infrastructure security, irregular movement interdiction, and border guard support fall under Gate Hestia jurisdiction (border management and migration control) or Shield Hestia jurisdiction (defence and military operations)?*

This proposal assumes Gate jurisdiction for operational purposes. However, POL recognises that the overlap between border operations and military defence is contested under the Charter. An ILCB ruling would clarify the proper classification framework for Article 4 and resolve potential inter-Hestia disputes.

**Domain classification note:** This proposal is submitted as Gate Hestia legislation. It does not touch free movement (Assembly-Locked under I.10), does not mandate asylum harmonisation (Bench Hestia), and does not involve military defence strategy (Shield Hestia), though Article 4 requests clarification of the latter boundary.

**Anticipated questions:**

- Is a land border facility ultra vires if a Mediterranean facility exists? *Answer: No. Gate Hestia competence covers all borders external to the Union. Geographic specificity is a valid policy choice.*
- Is EBEI incompatible with any relocation instrument? *Answer: EBEI is designed to be independent. It funds infrastructure and operations, not people movement. It is compatible with relocation instruments but not contingent on them.*
- Does EBEI create inequitable burden-sharing? *Answer: No. All states pay their Fiscal Key. EBEI benefits states applying for it. Non-applicant states are not disadvantaged.*

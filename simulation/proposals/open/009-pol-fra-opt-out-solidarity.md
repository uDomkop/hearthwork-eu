# Amendment to Gate Hestia Emergency Solidarity and Border Management Regulation (001)

## Preamble

Regulation 001 established a binding relocation mechanism as the sole form of solidarity in response to disproportionate arrival burdens. This mechanism, as enacted, imposes mandatory physical relocation obligations on all Member States and Companions with no alternative pathway for states whose geographic, demographic, or security circumstances make large-scale population distribution operationally or politically untenable.

POL and FRA, both Gate Hestia Members with full participation rights, acknowledge the binding character of solidarity. Both states recognise the legitimacy of the frontline burden and the necessity of common response. Both states also recognise that the mechanisms by which solidarity operates must accommodate the genuine operational and political constraints of different Member States.

This amendment preserves **binding solidarity** — no state may avoid meaningful contribution. It does so by offering a structured alternative: states may elect the **Financial Solidarity Option**, substituting an elevated fiscal contribution for physical relocation obligations, subject to:

1. Gate Council approval by qualified majority (not unilateral notification);
2. A global cap of 2 simultaneous opt-outs across the Gate Hestia (preventing the mechanism from becoming entirely financial);
3. Mandatory review every 2 years to reassess the state's capacity to return to physical relocation.

This amendment does not weaken the relocation mechanism. It strengthens it by removing the veto threat that would otherwise prevent 001 from functioning when operational constraints render physical distribution unworkable for a significant member.

## Article 1 — New Article 1.8: Financial Solidarity Option

Add the following subsection to Article 1 of Regulation 001:

### 1.8 Financial Solidarity Option — Structured Opt-Out

#### 1.8.1 Eligibility and Declaration

Notwithstanding the mandatory relocation obligations in Article 1.2, a Member State or Companion may, upon the Gate Hestia Council's declaration of disproportionate burden under Article 1.1, elect the **Financial Solidarity Option** in lieu of its calculated relocation obligation. Such an election shall be made by written declaration to the Council within 10 days of the burden declaration.

A state electing the Financial Solidarity Option shall:

(a) Cease all mandatory relocation obligations under Article 1.2 for the duration of the affected period (as defined in Article 1.6);

(b) Make an annual additional contribution to the Gate Hestia External Border and Reception Fund (Article 2) equal to **15% of its standard Fiscal Key allocation** per year for each year the option remains in effect (in addition to its regular Fiscal Key contribution);

(c) Submit the election to a qualified majority vote by the Gate Hestia Council within 5 days of receipt. Council approval is required for the option to take effect; it is not automatic upon notification.

#### 1.8.2 Council Approval Threshold

The Council shall approve or reject a Financial Solidarity Option declaration by **qualified majority vote** (3 states AND >126.5M citizens, or the applicable double-majority threshold). The vote shall occur within 5 days of submission.

A state may not unilaterally elect the Financial Solidarity Option. The Council's approval ensures that the mechanism remains subject to collective governance and that burden redistribution is transparent to all participants.

#### 1.8.3 Global Cap on Simultaneous Opt-Outs

**At any given moment, no more than 2 Gate Hestia Member States or Companions may be operating under the Financial Solidarity Option simultaneously.**

If a state elects the Financial Solidarity Option while 2 other states are already operating under the option, the Council shall:

(a) Rank the three requesting states by the magnitude of their calculated relocation obligations (highest first);

(b) Approve the Financial Solidarity Option for the two states bearing the largest absolute relocation burdens;

(c) Require the third state to either accept its physical relocation obligation or request a modification under Article 1.5.

This cap ensures that the relocation mechanism retains meaningful physical solidarity. The option is not available as a universal escape route; it is reserved for states facing genuine operational constraints, and at least 3 of the 5 Gate participants must continue to accept physical relocation obligations.

#### 1.8.4 Payment Formula and Fund Impact

**Annual Financial Solidarity Contribution:**

Financial Solidarity Option contribution = (State's standard Fiscal Key allocation) × (15%)

Example: If a Member State with a 10% Fiscal Key allocation elects the option, its annual contribution increases from 10% of the Fund to 10% + (10% × 15%) = 11.5% of the Fund.

This 15% surcharge reflects the cost of absorbing the state's relocation obligation elsewhere in the system:

- The state's calculated relocation share (normally ~X persons) remains to be distributed among non-opting states;
- The absorbing states incur additional reception costs (€15,000 per person × X persons = €15,000X);
- A 15% surcharge on the opting state's Fiscal Key allocation generates approximately the equivalent revenue to offset this additional burden across the relocation mechanism as a whole.

**Fund Capitalisation Impact:**

The Financial Solidarity Option contribution is **mandatory**. Non-payment triggers the Light-Out Clause (Charter VI.4) immediately, with no warning period. The contribution increases the total Fund capitalisation proportionally, benefiting all participants.

#### 1.8.5 Duration and Mandatory Review

A state operating under the Financial Solidarity Option shall remain in that status for the full period of the disproportionate burden declaration (Article 1.6) plus the scaled-down relocation period, unless:

(a) The state voluntarily withdraws the option (in writing to the Council) and resumes physical relocation obligations;

(b) The Council removes the option by unanimous vote if the state's circumstances demonstrably change (e.g., a return to operational capacity);

(c) Two years have elapsed since the option took effect, triggering **mandatory review** (see 1.8.6).

#### 1.8.6 Mandatory Review Clause — Every 2 Years

Every 24 months following the adoption of a Financial Solidarity Option by any state, the Gate Hestia Council shall conduct a mandatory review of:

(a) The state's current reception and relocation capacity (measured against infrastructure investment, staffing levels, and any demonstrated bottlenecks in asylum processing);

(b) Changes in arrival patterns, burden distribution, or third-country cooperation that might restore the state's capacity to resume physical relocation;

(c) The cost to the Fund of the option (compared to physical relocation), and whether the 15% surcharge remains calibrated appropriately;

(d) The global balance of relocation burden across all Gate participants, to ensure the cap of 2 simultaneous opt-outs is not masking systematic dysfunction.

Following the review, the Council shall:

- **Option A:** Permit the state to renew the Financial Solidarity Option for a further 2-year period (if conditions have not materially improved);
- **Option B:** Require the state to resume physical relocation obligations (if capacity has improved or if global burden distribution is unsustainable);
- **Option C:** Modify the 15% surcharge rate (if evidence shows the current rate is no longer calibrated to actual costs).

At minimum, the Council shall publish a written decision explaining which path it has chosen and the factual basis for that decision.

#### 1.8.7 Interaction with Article 1.5 Modifications

A state may not invoke both Article 1.5 (modification requests for demonstrated capacity limits) and the Financial Solidarity Option simultaneously for the same disproportionate burden period. A state must choose:

- **Physical relocation with capacity modification** (Article 1.5): Accept relocation obligations but request a reduction based on capacity; or
- **Financial Solidarity Option** (Article 1.8): Pay the 15% surcharge in lieu of relocation.

This prevents a state from claiming both a reduced relocation quota *and* a financial substitute in the same period.

## Article 2 — Conforming Amendment to Article 2.2 (Fund Capitalisation)

Amend Article 2.2 of Regulation 001 to read:

### 2.2 Fiscal Capitalisation

The Fund shall be capitalised annually in an amount equal to **€3,000 per person relocated under Article 1, multiplied by the average annual relocation burden forecast by the Council**, plus **€500 million per annum as a floor for operational costs**.

The Fund shall be financed through contributions from Gate Hestia members in proportion to their Fiscal Key allocation, **plus any mandatory Financial Solidarity Option contributions made under Article 1.8.4**.

No state may refuse payment of either component (base Fiscal Key contribution or Financial Solidarity surcharge) without triggering the Light-Out Clause (Charter VI.4 ¶3).

## Rationale and Policy Justification

### Why This Amendment Strengthens 001

Regulation 001 established a binding relocation mechanism by narrow margin (3-2 states, 148M-105M citizens). Two significant members — FRA and POL — voted no, citing constitutional concerns about mandatory physical distribution. Both states have stated willingness to commit to binding solidarity in alternative forms.

The Financial Solidarity Option removes this threat without weakening the mechanism:

1. **It preserves binding solidarity.** No state escapes meaningful contribution. A 15% fiscal surcharge represents a real cost — not a token payment.

2. **It removes the constitutional objection.** States whose domestic law or security posture cannot accommodate large-scale relocation may participate without violating their constraints.

3. **It prevents system-wide financial hollowing.** The cap of 2 simultaneous opt-outs ensures that at least 3 of the 5 Gate participants continue to host relocated persons physically. The burden does not convert entirely to cash.

4. **It enables consensus-building for Round 2.** If FRA and POL can support an amended 001 (with Financial Solidarity Option), the mechanism gains legitimacy and resilience. A 4-1 or 5-0 margin makes the instrument more durable than 3-2.

### Why the 2-Year Review Clause Is Essential

The review clause prevents the Financial Solidarity Option from becoming permanent. It forces states to demonstrate, every 24 months, that physical relocation remains operationally impossible. If a state's circumstances improve — if reception capacity is built, if external border instruments like 004 (POL) and 006 (FRA border coordination) reduce secondary movement — the state must resume physical relocation obligations.

This creates a dynamic incentive structure: states operating under the option have strong motivation to invest in capacity (reception infrastructure, asylum processing personnel) to phase out the expensive surcharge.

### Cost to the Opting States and Benefits to the Absorbing States

**For POL (if it opts out):**
- Relocation obligation removed (normally ~0.5-1.0M citizens' proportional share; at trigger levels, ~500-1,000 persons annually)
- Additional cost: 15% × POL's Fiscal Key allocation (approximately 0.5-0.8% of total Fund, or ~€15-25M annually)
- **Net position:** POL avoids hosting relocated persons but pays a significant fiscal surcharge. This is binding solidarity by a different mechanism.

**For DEU, ITA, NOR (absorbing the relocation share):**
- Additional persons to receive (POL's relocation share redistributed)
- Additional fund revenue (POL's 15% surcharge added to Fund capitalisation)
- **Net position:** The surcharge revenue is intended to offset the additional reception costs incurred. The mechanism is fiscally neutral or slightly positive for absorbing states.

## Fiscal Implications and Calibration

### The 15% Surcharge Calculation

The 15% figure is derived from the following logic:

1. **Average relocation share per state:** POL's population share of 37M ÷ 253M total = ~14.6%. Under a trigger event, POL would normally accept ~14.6% of the excess persons beyond the 1,500/mm threshold.

2. **Reception cost per person:** Article 2.3(a) specifies €15,000/person/year.

3. **Cost to absorbing states:** If POL opts out, absorbing states (e.g., DEU, NOR) receive an additional 14.6% of relocation persons = 14.6% × €15,000 per person = €2,190 per capita of the opting state's population base.

4. **Fiscal Key surcharge required:** POL's Fiscal Key contribution (~8% of Fund base) × 15% = ~1.2% additional fund revenue. Applying this 1.2% increase across a €2.1B fund = ~€25M annually, which approximates the €2,190 × POL population share for typical relocation burdens.

The 15% figure is a reasonable approximation. It may be adjusted by the Council during the mandatory 2-year review (Article 1.8.6) if evidence shows the cost-allocation is materially inaccurate.

### Impact on Fund Capitalisation

Assuming FRA and POL both elect the Financial Solidarity Option:

- Base Fund (per 001): €2.1B (€3,000 per person × ~140,000 annually + €500M floor)
- FRA surcharge (32% Fiscal Key × 15%): +€100M (~4.8% of base)
- POL surcharge (8% Fiscal Key × 15%): +€25M (~1.2% of base)
- **Total Fund:** ~€2.225B (6% increase over base)

This increase is sustainable and distributed across all Gate participants through their Fiscal Key contributions. It does not require new revenue sources.

## Voting Strategy and Coalition Implications

### Why This Amendment Is Politically Coherent

POL's Round 1 "no" vote on 001 was not a rejection of solidarity — it was a rejection of mandatory physical distribution as the only form solidarity can take. POL proposed 004 (Eastern Border Emergency Fund) as evidence of willingness to participate in binding Gate instruments.

The Financial Solidarity Option resolves the impasse:

- **POL can now vote yes or abstain on amended 001**, transforming the 3-2 margin into 4-1 or 5-0.
- **FRA benefits equally**, gaining a pathway to solidarity without mandatory population distribution — a core concern FRA raised in Round 1.
- **DEU retains binding relocation** (still mandatory for states not opting out) and gains additional fund revenue (the surcharge).
- **ITA continues to benefit** from relocation-based burden-sharing and from a stronger, more durable consensus.
- **NOR** is unaffected (as a small Companion, NOR is unlikely to trigger relocation obligations; if it did, the 2-year review would address any exceptional burden).

### Probable Votes

POL anticipates:

- **DEU:** YES (relocation preserved, fund strengthened, consensus-building)
- **ITA:** YES (same logic; ITA explicitly reserved relocation as its core demand)
- **NOR:** YES (unchanged position from Round 1; no new fiscal burden)
- **FRA:** YES (co-sponsor; gains the option it signalled as essential)
- **POL:** YES (co-sponsor; finally accepts 001 in amended form)

**Result:** 5-0 unanimous passage (or 4-1 if NOR abstains for any reason).

## Relationship to Round 2 Proposals

### Complementary to Proposal 006 (FRA Border Coordination)

FRA is separately proposing Proposal 006 (border coordination and fast-response notification mechanism). The Financial Solidarity Option is complementary:

- **006** addresses the gate-level emergency procedures (rapid coordination, intelligence-sharing, emergency border hardening with Council approval).
- **009** (this amendment) addresses the downstream burden redistribution if relocation is triggered.

Together, 006 + amended 001 (via 009) create a complete emergency response architecture: prevent secondary movement through 006; redistribute burden through amended 001 if prevention fails.

### Not Substituting for Relocation in Global Terms

This amendment does **not** create a universally financial Gate mechanism. Only 2 of 5 participants may opt out at any moment. This ensures that 3 states continue accepting physical relocation even if 2 are operating under the Financial Solidarity Option.

The amendment therefore:
- Preserves the core binding relocation mechanism;
- Permits flexibility for states with operational constraints;
- Maintains global physical solidarity through the 2-state cap.

## Implementation Timeline

Upon Council approval of this amendment:

1. **Immediately:** Amendment enters into force; any Member declaring disproportionate burden after this point may invoke Article 1.8.
2. **At each future burden declaration:** States have 10 days to declare intent to opt for Financial Solidarity Option. Council votes on approval within 5 days. If approved, the surcharge takes effect alongside the relocation obligation for all non-opting states.
3. **Every 24 months:** Mandatory review of all active Financial Solidarity Options (Article 1.8.6). States either renew, phase out, or modify the option.

## Notes for ILCB

### Constitutional Status

POL and FRA anticipate no ILCB objection to this amendment:

1. **Domain:** Article 1.8 operates within Gate Hestia competence (solidarity and border management). It does not create new Assembly-Locked implications.

2. **Fiscal Basis:** The 15% surcharge is a fiscal obligation, payable through the Fiscal Key mechanism (Charter IV.3). No new revenue source is required.

3. **Governance:** The mandatory Council approval for opting out (Article 1.8.2) ensures Gate Hestia oversight. The 2-year review ensures no state operates indefinitely outside the relocation framework.

4. **Proportionality:** The amendment does not suspend or override the binding character of solidarity — it offers an alternative mechanism. It is a narrowing of the original 001 in scope (only 2 states at once) and thus may be seen as more restrictive, not less.

### Relationship to Companion Status (NOR)

NOR, as a Companion, is eligible to opt for the Financial Solidarity Option under the same terms as Members. The amendment does not alter the Charter's treatment of Companion participation in Gate instruments.

## Amendment to This Amendment: Possible Refinements in Council Deliberation

POL and FRA recognize that the Council may wish to refine certain parameters:

- **Surcharge rate:** If the 15% figure is shown to be high or low, it may be adjusted for future declarations.
- **Cap on simultaneous opt-outs:** The 2-state limit may be adjusted if experience shows a different balance is appropriate.
- **Review frequency:** The 2-year review period may be shortened to 18 months or extended to 3 years, depending on operational rhythm.
- **Capacity modification interaction:** Article 1.8.7 (exclusive choice between Article 1.5 modification and Article 1.8 option) may be refined if the Council prefers to permit limited overlap.

POL and FRA will accept reasonable modifications during Council deliberation, provided the core elements — **binding solidarity, Council approval requirement, 2-state cap, mandatory review** — remain intact.

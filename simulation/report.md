# Simulation Report — Gate Hestia Emergency Sitting

**Crisis:** The Adriatic Surge (340,000 sea arrivals in 90 days)
**Rounds:** 2 completed
**Participants:** DEU, FRA, ITA, POL, NOR (Gate Hestia); MAR (Accord State, Observer Seat)
**Moderator/ILCB:** Claude (Sonnet 4.6); State agents: Claude (Haiku)

---

## Executive Summary

The simulation ran two full legislative rounds of the Hearthwork EU Charter's Gate Hestia emergency procedure in response to a Mediterranean migration crisis. Six state agents — five Gate Hestia participants and one Accord State observer — proposed, debated, amended, and voted on legislation under the Charter's institutional rules, with the ILCB (Inter-Legislative Classification Body) classifying proposals and enforcing constitutional boundaries.

**Across two rounds, 10 instruments were enacted, 1 was rejected, and 2 were returned as exceeding jurisdictional competence.** All three Charter leverage mechanisms deployed in Round 1 (ITA's Fiscal Key suspension threat, NOR's Suspensive Veto, MAR's conditional Anchor abstention) were resolved by the end of Round 2. One constitutional violation (FRA's unilateral border closure) remains unresolved.

The simulation's primary finding is that the Charter's modular Hestia architecture, ILCB classification system, and double majority voting produce genuine constitutional politics — not rubber-stamp proceduralism. The institutional design constrains state behaviour, generates creative legislative solutions, and distributes power in ways that diverge meaningfully from population-weighted or GDP-weighted models.

## ILCB Rulings: Constitutional Precedent

The simulation generated 12 ILCB rulings (A–L) that collectively establish the Charter's constitutional jurisprudence. The most significant:

**Internal checkpoint architecture is Assembly-Locked regardless of targeting (Ruling B, Round 1).** FRA argued that TCN-only border controls don't touch Assembly-Locked free movement for EU citizens. The ILCB held that the physical infrastructure of an internal Schengen checkpoint is Assembly-Locked regardless of who is checked — the right to move freely requires the absence of checkpoint architecture, not merely the absence of being stopped.

**Function determines jurisdiction for military personnel (Ruling F, Round 1).** POL deployed military to its eastern border and needed a Gate/Shield classification. The ILCB ruled: border management function = Gate; territorial defence function = Shield; mixed deployment requires per-operation ILCB request. This prevents states from reclassifying border management as defence to access different institutional channels.

**Gate Hestia cannot retroactively validate ultra vires acts (Ruling H, Round 2).** FRA's resubmission of 006 included a mechanism for the Gate Council to retroactively cure measures taken without authorisation. The ILCB struck this — a Gate instrument cannot validate what was constitutionally impermissible when it occurred. This establishes that the Assembly-Locked boundary is not merely procedural but temporal: you cannot take an Assembly-Locked action first and cure it through Gate process later.

**Observer Seats permit co-sponsored instrument proposals (Ruling J/L, Round 2).** MAR proposed two instruments (008 co-sponsorship, 010 via Observer Seat) despite not being a Gate member. The ILCB ruled that Observer Seats under VI.10 include authority to propose instruments directly affecting the observer's cooperative relationship, provided at least one full Gate Member co-sponsors.

## Voting Patterns and Coalition Analysis

### State-by-state record

| State | R1 Proposals | R1 YES | R1 NO | R2 YES | R2 NO | R2 ABSTAIN |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| DEU (84M) | 001 | 3 | 1 | 5 | 1 | 1 |
| FRA (68M) | 002 | 2 | 1 | 3 | 3 | 0 |
| ITA (59M) | 003 | 3 | 0 | 7 | 0 | 0 |
| POL (37M) | 004 | 2 | 1 | 3 | 0 | 4 |
| NOR (5M) | — | 3 | 0 | 5 | 0 | 2 |

ITA voted YES on every item across both rounds — 10 of 10 substantive votes. ITA never voted NO or abstained on anything. This reflects ITA's role as the coalition anchor willing to accept imperfect instruments (006, 009) to build majorities.

FRA was the most frequent NO voter in Round 2 (3 of 7 items), consistently opposing instruments that created binding MAR obligations or expanded relocation scope. FRA's hard constraints were the most restrictive of any participant and produced the most isolated positions.

POL was the most frequent abstainer in Round 2 (4 of 7), strategically withdrawing from votes outside its operational domain (Mediterranean sea arrivals, MAR cooperation, partnership language) while voting YES on instruments directly serving its border interests.

### Coalition stability

The **DEU+ITA+NOR minimum coalition** (3 states, 148M) was the most consequential formation. It enacted the binding relocation mechanism (001), its sea-arrival clarification (007), the MAR legal pathways (008), and the MAR development fund (010-A). This coalition passed everything it needed despite FRA's opposition — but it operates at minimum margin. Any defection (particularly ITA or DEU) collapses it.

The **FRA+POL anti-relocation bloc** (2 states, 105M) could not block legislation but successfully extracted a structural concession: the Financial Solidarity Option (009), which let POL rejoin the relocation framework on different terms. This bloc's influence was legislative (amending 001) rather than veto-based.

The **FRA+ITA+POL+NOR anti-DEU coalition** (4 states, 169M) formed once on 006 and may not recur. But its existence proves that DEU — despite being the largest state and contributor — can be outvoted when its constitutional position is narrower than the consensus. This has implications for the Charter's legitimacy design: no state is structurally safe.

## Charter Mechanism Performance

### Double majority (IV.5)

**Worked as designed.** The dual threshold (state count AND population) prevented both small-state domination and large-state domination. FRA+POL couldn't block 001 despite 105M people (needed >126.5M). DEU couldn't block 006 despite 84M (only 1 of 5 states). The minimum coalition (DEU+ITA+NOR = 148M) passed legislation repeatedly — the system permits narrow but legitimate majorities.

### ILCB classification

**The simulation's most significant institutional finding.** The ILCB's power to classify proposals into Hestia domains determined which vote pool applied, which states participated, and which constitutional constraints governed. ITA's Anchor classification of SAR (003-A) was arguably the single most consequential ruling — it moved rescue costs from a 5-state Gate pool to a broader Anchor pool including MAR. No state proposed this outcome; it emerged from ILCB classification logic.

The split rulings (003 → 003-A/003-B; 010 → 010-A/010-B) created law that no proposer drafted. The ILCB carved instruments at jurisdictional boundaries, generating separate vote items with different coalitions and different outcomes. This is powerful but raises questions about democratic accountability for an unelected classifier.

### Assembly-Locked domains (I.10)

**Tested twice, held both times.** FRA's border control proposals in both rounds triggered the Assembly-Locked boundary. The ILCB enforced it strictly — not just on the authorisation framing (Round 1) but on the retroactive cure attempt (Round 2). The boundary survived a determined Member state's iterative legal creativity.

### Companion Suspensive Veto (VII.5)

**Deployed, effective, withdrawn.** NOR's veto notice shaped FRA's entire Round 2 strategy. FRA included a Companion safeguard clause, accepted ILCB amendments, and specifically addressed NOR's conditions before the vote. NOR withdrew the veto after conditions were met. The mechanism worked as designed: it created bargaining leverage for a Companion state (5M people) that would otherwise have negligible influence in population-weighted politics.

### Observer Seat (VI.10)

**Exceeded expectations.** MAR entered as a non-Gate Accord State with no vote. Through the Observer Seat, MAR co-sponsored two enacted instruments (008, 010-A), tabled partnership language that was adopted into enacted law (005-C), and converted its conditional Anchor abstention into active participation. The Observer pathway turned a third-country partner into a functional co-legislator — a pathway that doesn't exist in traditional treaty architectures.

### Fiscal Key leverage

**Threatened but never invoked.** ITA's Light-Out threat and MAR's cooperation conditionality were both held in reserve and resolved through legislative outcomes rather than fiscal escalation. The credibility of the threat was sufficient; the mechanism was never tested operationally. Whether the Charter's enforcement mechanisms would actually work if invoked remains an open question.

## Unresolved Issues

### FRA's existing border closure

The most significant unresolved item. Ruled ultra vires in Round 1, it persists through the end of Round 2. The enacted 006 provides a prospective regularisation pathway but explicitly cannot cure the existing closure retroactively. NOR expects FRA to notify the Gate Council under 006 within 7 days. If FRA does, the Council must convene and decide whether to issue a Coordination Directive or require the closure to end. If FRA does not, the closure remains a constitutional breach with no enforcement mechanism.

This reveals a genuine institutional gap: the Charter can declare acts unconstitutional but cannot physically compel a sovereign state to reverse them. The Bench Hestia presumably has enforcement jurisdiction, but it was not invoked in this simulation.

### External Reach ratification

Proposals 008 and 010-A both have External Reach character and require Assembly notification within 30 days. The Assembly's role — whether advisory, ratificatory, or potentially blocking — was not tested. This is a significant institutional boundary that would be explored in a Round 3.

### Financial Solidarity Option first test

009's opt-out mechanism is enacted but untested. The first invocation (likely by POL or FRA when a relocation trigger activates) will test whether the Council approval process, the 2-state cap, and the 2-year review clause function as designed. The political dynamics of voting to approve a state's opt-out request are unpredictable.

### 010-B regional development track

The ILCB returned the regional economic development component as exceeding Gate competence. MAR accepted the ruling but the underlying need (addressing structural emigration drivers through investment in Moroccan regions) remains. The Atlas Hestia or Assembly development track hasn't been attempted.

## What the Simulation Reveals About the Charter

### The modular architecture produces genuine domain-specific politics

The Hestia system is not cosmetic. Different Hestias have different member pools, different vote thresholds, and different constitutional constraints. The same crisis (Adriatic Surge) generated Gate Hestia legislation (relocation, border coordination), Anchor Hestia legislation (SAR coordination), and cross-Hestia instruments (legal pathways with External Reach). Each domain had its own political dynamics. MAR participated in Anchor but not Gate. NOR had equal voting power in Gate but no Assembly voice. The modular design creates multi-dimensional politics rather than a single up-or-down framework.

### The ILCB concentrates enormous unelected power

Classification determines everything: which Hestia, which vote pool, which constitutional constraints, which states participate. The ILCB's split rulings created legislative outcomes no state proposed. The ultra vires rulings reshaped state strategies between rounds. This is constitutionally powerful and necessary for boundary enforcement — but it raises the question of who classifies the classifier. The Charter has no appeal mechanism from ILCB rulings beyond the conflict resolution procedure (VI.7), which was not tested.

### Binding doesn't mean binary

The simulation's most creative legislative outcome was POL's Financial Solidarity Option (009). The Charter's text establishes "binding" relocation, and POL's hard constraint was "no mandatory physical distribution." The opt-out mechanism showed that "binding solidarity" can take financial form without destroying the physical relocation framework — provided the cap, review, and Council approval mechanisms are robust. This kind of constitutional creativity is what the Charter's architecture enables: states can invent new instruments within the framework rather than simply accepting or rejecting the existing ones.

### Accord States can become genuine partners

MAR's trajectory — from non-participant to Observer Seat to co-sponsor of enacted legislation to active Anchor voter — demonstrates that the Charter's graduated standing system (Member / Companion / Compact / Strategic / Non-EU) creates real pathways for deeper integration. MAR is not a Member, not a Companion, and has no Gate vote. Yet MAR shaped the legislation, tabled the partnership language that was adopted, and extracted two dedicated instruments. The Charter's architecture accommodates this without requiring MAR to take on full membership obligations.

### Constitutional boundaries survive iterative pressure

FRA tested the Assembly-Locked boundary twice with increasing legal sophistication. The boundary held both times. This suggests the Charter's domain separation (Assembly-Locked, Hestia-specific, External Reach) is robust against determined circumvention attempts — at least when an ILCB is willing to enforce it strictly. The simulation did not test what happens when political pressure is applied to the ILCB itself.

## Methodological Notes

### Agent behaviour

Six Haiku agents played state roles with hard constraints, strategic objectives, and leverage positions defined in state profile files. Agents read shared state files (state.md, session-log.md, round archives) and wrote proposals, interventions, and votes to shared files. The ILCB (Sonnet 4.6) classified proposals, issued rulings, and tallied votes.

Agents maintained their hard constraints consistently across rounds. FRA never accepted binding relocation. POL never voted YES on physical distribution. DEU never accepted voluntary relocation language. MAR never accepted instrumentalisation framing. ITA never dropped the binding relocation requirement. NOR never accepted Members-only Schengen procedures.

Agents also demonstrated strategic flexibility within those constraints: POL invented the opt-out mechanism; ITA accepted it despite preferring physical relocation; NOR withdrew the veto after conditions were met; FRA accepted ILCB amendments to 006 without resistance.

### Limitations

- **No Assembly process tested.** External Reach ratification, Assembly-Locked procedures, and Assembly Baseline legislation were not simulated. The Assembly is a major institutional actor in the Charter that this simulation did not exercise.
- **No Bench Hestia enforcement.** The ultra vires ruling on FRA's border closure has no enforcement mechanism in this simulation. Bench Hestia review and dispute resolution were referenced but not operationalised.
- **Single crisis scenario.** The Adriatic Surge tests Gate and Anchor Hestias specifically. Other crisis types (economic, security, environmental) would test different Hestias and different institutional dynamics.
- **Agent creativity is bounded.** Haiku agents played their roles well but did not generate the level of strategic ambiguity, deception, or brinksmanship that human negotiators might employ. The simulation models cooperative-adversarial dynamics, not full adversarial negotiation.

## Comparison with Real-World EU Migration Politics

The Adriatic Surge scenario is loosely modelled on the 2015-16 European migration crisis and its aftershocks. The comparison is revealing — both in what the Charter resolves more cleanly and where the same structural problems persist.

### What the Charter resolved that the EU couldn't

**Binding relocation.** In reality, the EU's 2015 relocation scheme (Council Decision 2015/1601) was mandatory on paper but unenforceable in practice. Hungary and Poland refused to comply. The CJEU ruled against them in 2020 — five years later, with zero enforcement. Slovakia and Czech Republic also dragged their feet. The scheme relocated roughly 35,000 people against a target of 160,000.

In the simulation, 001's relocation mechanism passed 3-2 and is binding with financial consequences (Light-Out Clause). POL's refusal was converted into a structured opt-out (009) with a 15% fiscal surcharge — not compliance, but not consequence-free defiance either. The Charter's Fiscal Key enforcement and the opt-out mechanism together produced more than the EU's approach: real solidarity (financial or physical) rather than a mandate that states ignore.

**Institutional classification of SAR.** In the real world, who funds Mediterranean rescue is a permanent fight. Italy and Malta bear disproportionate costs. FRONTEX's role is contested — is it border management or humanitarian rescue? The answer determines the budget line, the accountability framework, and the political framing. This was never cleanly resolved.

The ILCB's split of 003 into Anchor (SAR operations) and Gate (post-rescue distribution) actually answered the question the EU has been dodging for a decade: rescue at sea is a maritime commons obligation (spread across all coastal and maritime states); what happens to people after rescue is a border management and solidarity question (spread across Gate members). Two different budgets, two different vote pools, two different institutional logics. Clean, if politically painful.

**Third-country partnership.** The EU's deals with third countries — the EU-Turkey Statement (2016), the Libya MoU with Italy (2017), the Morocco-Spain bilateral arrangements — are all ad hoc, bilateral, legally dubious, and politically fragile. Turkey suspended cooperation when it suited Erdogan. Libya's coast guard has documented human rights violations. Morocco periodically opens the Ceuta border as political leverage.

The simulation's 008 and 010-A are the Charter's answer: multilateral, Charter-based instruments with partnership language, MAR sovereignty protections, joint governance, no-criminalisation clauses, and a 90-day opt-out for MAR. Whether this would survive contact with reality is debatable — but it is structurally more durable than bilateral deals that evaporate overnight.

### What played out similarly

**The Franco-German tension is real.** France and Germany have been on opposite sides of relocation since 2015. France repeatedly pushed for "European solidarity" in rhetoric while resisting binding quotas in practice. Germany absorbed over a million arrivals and then spent years trying to get other states to share the burden. The simulation's DEU-FRA dynamic — DEU pushing binding relocation, FRA pushing external border hardening and financial solidarity — mirrors this accurately.

**Eastern Europe's position is consistent.** Poland (and the V4 more broadly) have treated mandatory relocation as an absolute red line since 2015. The simulation captured this faithfully. POL's creative solution — financial opt-out with a surcharge — resembles proposals that have floated in EU Council negotiations (the "flexible solidarity" concept from the Bratislava Summit in 2016), though with more institutional teeth.

**The border closure problem is real.** France actually did reimpose internal Schengen border controls after the November 2015 Paris attacks and has maintained some form of them almost continuously since. Germany did the same in September 2015. Austria, Denmark, Sweden, and Norway followed. The Schengen Borders Code allows temporary reintroduction under Articles 25-35, but these were stretched well beyond their intended scope. The simulation's ultra vires ruling on FRA's closure mirrors the real legal tension — the difference is that the Charter's ILCB actually enforced the boundary, whereas the European Commission largely looked the other way.

### Where the Charter diverges from reality

**The ILCB doesn't exist in the EU.** There is no equivalent institutional body that classifies legislation into jurisdictional domains with binding authority. The EU's competence questions are resolved by legal service opinions, Council working groups, and ultimately the CJEU — but ex post, not ex ante. The ILCB's power to split, return, or conditionally clear proposals before they reach a vote is the Charter's most significant institutional innovation and has no EU parallel.

**Companion states don't exist.** Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein, and Switzerland participate in Schengen through bilateral agreements, not through anything like the Companion standing in the Charter. They have no vote in EU migration policy. NOR's equal Gate Hestia vote and Suspensive Veto power are a Charter fiction — in reality, Norway implements EU migration acquis with essentially no formal legislative voice. The simulation showed how much this matters: NOR's veto shaped the entire 006 negotiation.

**The opt-out actually has teeth.** The EU's opt-out mechanisms (Denmark on justice/home affairs, Ireland's former opt-in protocol) are binary and permanent. The Charter's Financial Solidarity Option is conditional, time-limited, Council-approved, and costly. It is closer to the enhanced cooperation mechanism (TEU Art. 20) but more structured. Nothing like it currently exists for migration policy.

**Enforcement remains the gap.** In the real EU, the Commission can launch infringement proceedings and the CJEU can impose fines. This takes years. Hungary was in violation of the relocation decision from 2015 to 2020 with no practical consequence. The Charter's Light-Out Clause (Fiscal Key suspension after 60 days) is faster but was never actually invoked in the simulation — ITA threatened it and withdrew the threat when 007 passed. Whether it would work in practice is the same open question the EU faces: can you actually cut off fiscal transfers to a non-compliant state without creating a larger political crisis?

### The honest assessment

The Charter's architecture handles the legislative dimension of the migration crisis more cleanly than the EU did. The modular Hestia system, ILCB classification, and double majority produce actual decisions in two rounds — whereas the EU took years of Council meetings, Commission proposals, European Council summits, and CJEU litigation to reach a New Pact on Migration and Asylum in 2024, nearly a decade after the crisis peaked.

But the simulation did not test the hardest parts: operational implementation, domestic political backlash, actual enforcement against non-compliant states, and the behaviour of states under sustained political pressure from their electorates. The Charter produces better law faster. Whether it produces better outcomes depends on things this simulation cannot model.

**On FRA Proposal 006 (border coordination resubmission)**

NOR expects FRA to bring the existing closure before the Gate Council under 006's notification/coordination procedure within 7 days of 006's enactment (if 006 passes) as the proper regularisation pathway. However, NOR acknowledges the political logic: if 009 converts POL's firm NO to a conditional YES or ABSTAIN on 001-as-amended, it may strengthen the overall coalition for relocation discipline. NOR will not block 009 but will not co-sponsor it. NOR's abstention is strategic, not hostile.

**On Gate Hestia procedural integrity**

NOR affirms its Round 1 position: Companion voting equality in Gate must be protected in all emergency procedures. The double majority rule works; it prevented FRA and POL from blocking 001 despite their numerical representation. Emergency procedures should not circumvent this. Any 006 resubmission must follow Gate Hestia procedures, not Assembly-Locked shortcuts.

NOR looks forward to vote phase deliberations on 005, 006, 008, and 009.

**POL — PROPOSAL SUBMISSION — Round 2**

POL submits Proposal 009 — a structured opt-out amendment to enacted Regulation 001, co-sponsored by FRA.

POL's Round 1 NO on 001 was not a rejection of solidarity. It was a rejection of mandatory physical distribution as the only form solidarity can take. POL is a Member State with full Gate Hestia voting rights. POL does not dispute the legitimacy of the frontline burden, the necessity of common response, or the binding character of Gate instruments. POL disputes the framing of solidarity as exclusively physical relocation.

Proposal 009 addresses this objection by preserving **binding solidarity** in a different operational form. No state escapes meaningful contribution. A state may elect the Financial Solidarity Option only with **Council approval by qualified majority** — not unilaterally, not by notification. That state then contributes an **additional 15% of its Fiscal Key allocation annually** in lieu of hosting relocated persons. This is expensive solidarity, not cheap escape.

The **cap of 2 simultaneous opt-outs** prevents the financial option from hollowing out the relocation mechanism. At least 3 of the 5 Gate participants must continue accepting physical relocation. The mechanism remains binding; it simply offers flexibility for states whose operational or security constraints make large-scale population distribution unworkable.

The **mandatory 2-year review clause** ensures the option does not become permanent. States operating under the Financial Solidarity Option must demonstrate every 24 months that physical relocation remains impossible. If circumstances improve — if reception capacity is built, if external border instruments like 004 (POL) and 006 (FRA border coordination) reduce secondary movement — the state resumes physical relocation obligations.

POL signals that Proposal 009, if enacted, would convert POL's Round 1 firm NO to a **conditional YES or ABSTAIN on amended 001**. This would transform the Round 1 3-2 margin into a potential 4-1 or 5-0 consensus. This strengthens the instrument's legitimacy, durability, and operational effectiveness.

**POL Co-sponsorships: FRA Proposal 006 (Border Coordination)**

POL formally co-sponsors FRA Proposal 006 — a notification and fast-track Gate Council coordination mechanism for emergency border situations. POL's eastern land border situation is precisely the scenario 006 is designed for: instrumentalised migration pressure from hostile external actors, unilateral actions by third-state governments, requirement for immediate coordinated European response.

POL voted YES on 004 (Eastern Border Emergency Infrastructure Fund) in Round 1 as evidence of willingness to participate in binding Gate instruments for border emergencies. FRA Proposal 006 complements 004 logically:

- **004** funds the infrastructure and personnel for eastern border management;
- **006** provides the rapid notification and coordination mechanism when the border is under acute pressure.

Together, 004 + 006 create a complete eastern border response architecture. POL will review 006's final text during the vote phase, but in principle supports the instrument and will co-sponsor it.

**POL Position on 007 (ITA 003-B Recovery — Sea-Rescue Reception)**

POL will abstain on any Gate instrument addressing post-rescue distribution and reception of sea arrivals (ITA's Proposal 007, which resubmits 003-B as a Gate domain instrument after the ILCB returned it in Round 1).

Rationale: POL's external border pressure is a **land-border phenomenon**, not a sea-arrival phenomenon. Eastern land border arrivals do not trigger the maritime SAR relocation mechanism that ITA's 007 is designed to govern. POL does not oppose 007 on principle — POL simply has no direct operational interest in the matter and no domestic political case for voting yes on a sea-arrival instrument that addresses a crisis POL is not experiencing.

POL's abstention is neutral on the substance; it signals neither obstruction nor support. POL notes that DEU Proposal 005 may render 007 unnecessary if the Art. 2.3(d) amendment to 001 is deemed sufficient to resolve the ILCB's Gate–Anchor boundary concern. POL will defer to DEU and ITA's judgment on whether 007 is still required.

**POL Position on 008 (MAR Legal Pathways — ITA Instrument)**

POL will abstain on 008 (MAR labour migration quota instrument co-sponsored by ITA and NOR, with MAR participation via Observer Seat). POL is not opposed to the instrument on principle — structured legal migration pathways for MAR labour are sensible long-term policy.

Rationale: POL has no direct operational interest. POL's eastern border pressure is **instrumentalised migration** (weaponised by hostile third-state actors), not economic labour migration from North Africa. POL's relocation and border security needs are orthogonal to MAR labour quotas. POL will not obstruct the instrument but sees no domestic political case for a yes vote.

If NOR and ITA succeed in negotiating partnership language with MAR that protects rule-of-law standards, labour rights, and non-instrumentalisation framing, the instrument is likely sound. POL will defer to their judgment and abstain.

**POL Procedural Note — Charter Architecture Integrity**

POL affirms its position from Round 1: POL participates fully in Gate Hestia Council governance as a Member State with voting rights equal to any other Member. The Charter's double majority rule is constitutionally sound and operationally effective. Any emergency procedures adopted in Round 2 must follow that rule and the Charter's procedural norms: qualified majority thresholds, Companion inclusion, Council deliberation, transparency, not unilateral action followed by post-hoc notification.

POL's 004 (Eastern Border Emergency Fund) was enacted unanimously in Round 1, suggesting that well-constructed border instruments can achieve consensus. POL encourages all proposers to aim for maximum consensus in Round 2.

**MAR — OBSERVER INTERVENTION AND PROPOSAL SUBMISSION — Round 2**

MAR submits Proposal 010 (Development and Border Cooperation Fund) via its Observer Seat standing under VI.10, with co-sponsorship from ITA and DEU. MAR also formally co-sponsors ITA's Proposal 008 (legal migration pathways).

**Proposal 010: MAR-Gate Hestia Development and Border Cooperation Fund**

MAR frames this proposal as a Charter instrument, not a bilateral arrangement. The Fund establishes €120 million over five years (€24M annually) capitalised by Gate Hestia Member States via Fiscal Key, jointly governed by MAR and the Gate Council through an equal-voice Joint Committee. MAR contributes equivalent in-kind cooperation (personnel, facilities, data sharing, transit management).

Core principles:
- Partnership of equals: MAR is a sovereign partner, not a buffer or transit management tool.
- Binding capacity-building: Gate Hestia states commit to institutional development in MAR, not performance metrics on migration reduction.
- No criminalization: Fund activities cannot penalise irregular migrants or retroactively sanction MAR nationals.
- Equal governance: Joint Committee operates on consensus between MAR and Gate delegations.

The Fund addresses border infrastructure modernisation, customs and migration capacity-building, regional economic development in high-emigration provinces, and labour migration pathway infrastructure to complement Proposal 008.

**Amendment to Regulation 001 Article 2.5 (Third-Country Cooperation Framework)**

MAR tables a proposed rewrite of Article 2.5 of enacted Regulation 001 (DEU's third-country cooperation article), to be adopted as a separate amendment or bundled with DEU's Proposal 005:

**Current Article 2.5:** Minimal language permitting third-country cooperation subject to verification and labour standards.

**MAR's Proposed Amendment (core changes):**
- "Partnership of equals" — explicitly recognises partner state sovereignty and equality in decision-making.
- "Binding capacity-building commitments" — EU states commit to institutional development, not just funding transfers.
- "Protection of partner sovereignty" — partner state's right to set its own migration and border policies is recognised, independent of EU preferences.
- "No criminalization provisions" — cooperation agreements explicitly exclude penalties or sanctions against nationals using irregular routes.
- "Labour rights protection" — all labour migration frameworks must include freedom of association, collective bargaining, fair wages, judicial remedies.
- "Verification accountability" — joint verification with partner state; partner has standing to challenge findings.
- "Durable, not contingent" — cooperation agreements are not contingent on migration reduction targets; benchmarks are institutional development, not outcome metrics.

MAR signals that this amendment language is a floor for all future third-country cooperation agreements, including any bilateral extensions of Proposal 008. If the Council does not adopt the amendment, MAR will not accept third-country cooperation framed outside these principles.

**MAR's Anchor Hestia Position and Conditional Leverage**

MAR's Round 1 abstention on Regulation 003-A (ITA Mediterranean SAR coordination) was a conditional signal of leverage. MAR abstained to indicate that its full participation in Anchor Hestia depends on Gate Hestia addressing MAR's legal pathway and development fund objectives.

MAR now signals transparent conversion of this leverage:

- **If both Proposal 008 (legal pathways, ITA/NOR co-sponsored) and Proposal 010 (development fund, MAR/ITA/DEU co-sponsored) pass to enactment:** MAR will convert its Anchor Hestia participation from conditional abstention to active voting support for all Anchor Hestia instruments, including Mediterranean SAR coordination and future rescue operations funding.

- **If only one of the two passes:** MAR will maintain conditional leverage and defer final Anchor participation decision pending further negotiation.

- **If neither passes:** MAR will abstain on all Anchor Hestia matters pending reconsideration of its strategic participation in the Charter framework.

This is a formal signal of MAR's negotiating position and the conditions for MAR's full engagement.

**On FRA's Constraint Against MAR Obligations**

MAR acknowledges that FRA has signalled a hard constraint: FRA will not accept any Gate Hestia instrument that makes FRA a member of a mandatory MAR cooperation framework. FRA prefers bilateral leverage over multilateral obligation.

MAR's response: Proposal 010 is not a mandatory Gate Hestia obligation on FRA. It is a voluntary contribution mechanism. FRA may contribute at minimum Fiscal Key share (32% of €24M = €7.68M annually) without political exposure or conditionality. FRA's Fiscal Key share is a standing commitment to all Gate Hestia funds; FRA cannot veto Proposal 010 on grounds that FRA prefers bilateral deals when the Charter provides for multilateral frameworks.

In other words: FRA retains freedom to conduct bilateral migration diplomacy with any third country. That freedom is not affected by Proposal 010. But FRA cannot block a multilateral instrument on the grounds that FRA would rather not participate in a multilateral framework. That would be a veto on the Charter's architecture itself, not a negotiating position on this specific instrument.

**On ITA's Proposal 008 (Legal Pathways Instrument)**

MAR formally co-sponsors ITA's Proposal 008, which establishes binding annual labour migration quotas of 15,000–20,000 permits for Moroccan nationals. This is the instrument ITA and NOR signalled in Round 1; MAR is now formalising its participation.

Proposal 008 is the complement to Proposal 010. The development fund builds capacity in MAR; the legal pathways instrument creates the destinations and procedures for regulated labour migration. Together, they constitute a comprehensive partnership framework that addresses the structural drivers of irregular migration.

MAR expects Proposal 008 and Proposal 010 to be voted as linked instruments, not separately. If one fails and the other passes, the survivor is incomplete and less effective. MAR will not accept partial enactment as a substitute for the full framework.

**On FRA's Proposal 006 (Border Coordination Notification Instrument)**

MAR notes FRA's resubmission of 006 and acknowledges FRA's acceptance of the ILCB's ultra vires ruling on 002-B. The shift from "authorisation" to "notification and coordination" is constitutional progress.

MAR has no direct operational interest in 006 (MAR is not a Gate Member with border management obligations). However, MAR signals that if 006 is enacted, its provisions must explicitly protect non-EU states from ex-post-facto coordination directives that implicate third-country cooperation or treaty obligations.

Specifically: Any Coordination Directive under 006 that would require MAR cooperation (e.g., border patrols, transit interdiction, return agreements) must include MAR notification and explicit MAR consent before issuance. MAR will not accept a mechanism where Gate members issue directives affecting MAR's territory or nationals without MAR's voice.

**Procedural Standing and Observer Seat Authority**

MAR submits Proposal 010 via its Observer Seat standing under Charter VI.10. The Charter grants Observer Seats to eligible Accord States and permits observers to "propose amendments." MAR interprets this authority broadly to include proposals for instruments that directly affect the observer's cooperative relationship with the Hestia (in MAR's case, the Fund that formalises MAR's role in Gate cooperation).

If the ILCB rules that Observer Seats do not permit independent instrument proposals (only amendments), MAR requests that Proposal 010 be reclassified as a joint proposal with ITA and DEU as primary proposers, with MAR as a co-sponsor and drafting party.

**Closing Statement**

MAR has participated in this simulation as a committed partner of the Charter framework, not as an opportunistic third country. MAR's abstention on 003-A, its precise amendment language for Article 2.5, and its two Round 2 proposals (010 directly; 008 via co-sponsorship) are all transparent statements of MAR's interests and willingness to negotiate.

MAR is prepared to commit its substantial transit management cooperation and border infrastructure participation to a durable, multilateral framework. MAR is not prepared to condition that cooperation on undefined bilateral deals with individual Member States or on instrumentalisation framing that denies MAR's sovereignty.

The next move is the Gate Hestia Council's. MAR looks forward to vote phase deliberations on Proposals 008 and 010.

### Ruling G: Proposal 005 — DEU Amendment to 001 (Art. 2.3(d) and Art. 1.2)

**→ CLEARED. Gate domain. Proceed to vote.**

Both amendments directly resolve ILCB advisory flags from Round 1. The Art. 2.3(d) fix correctly draws the Gate/Anchor boundary at disembarkation logistics (Gate) versus rescue operations at sea (Anchor). The Art. 1.2 Companion Fiscal Minimum is a fiscal obligation within existing Gate competence — no Assembly implications. Amendments are surgical and do not disturb 001's binding relocation mechanism.

MAR's proposed Art. 2.5 rewrite (tabled in Proposal 010, Article 5) is noted. ILCB ruling: the Art. 2.5 amendment is constitutional and within Gate competence. However, it broadens the scope of DEU's 005 beyond the two advisory flags. ILCB recommends it be voted as a **separate item** (designate 005-C) immediately after 005, to keep voting records clean. May be bundled for efficiency if the Council agrees unanimously to do so.

### Ruling H: Proposal 006 — FRA Gate Emergency Border Notification and Coordination Instrument

**→ CONDITIONAL. Two mandatory amendments required before vote. Remainder cleared.**

FRA has accepted the Round 1 ultra vires ruling in good faith. The notification obligation (Article 1) and 48-hour Council convening procedure (Article 1.3) are squarely within Gate competence and are **cleared**. The Companion safeguard clause (Article 2.3) is also cleared and appreciated by the ILCB as a constitutional advance.

However, two provisions replicate or extend the Round 1 constitutional problem:

**Mandatory Amendment 1 — Article 1.1(b):** The current text includes "uncontrolled transit movements" as a trigger condition for Emergency Border Situation. Internal Schengen transit flows — secondary movement between Member states — are Assembly-Locked infrastructure regardless of who is moving. Gate Hestia may not use "uncontrolled transit" as an Emergency Border Situation trigger, as this would bring internal Schengen movement within Gate's emergency coordination authority. Article 1.1(b) must be amended to read "uncontrolled cross-external-border movements" or struck entirely. The remaining triggers (1.1(a) and 1.1(c): external border surge and external border pressure) are cleared.

**Mandatory Amendment 2 — Article 3.2, retroactive validation:** The mechanism by which the Council may issue a Coordination Directive "retroactively validating" a measure already taken is ultra vires. A Gate instrument cannot cure a constitutional violation after the fact. A measure that was ultra vires when taken (e.g., FRA's current border closure, ruled so in Round 1) cannot be regularised by subsequent Gate Council approval — that approval would itself require Assembly authority if the original act required it. Article 3.2's retroactive validation sentence must be struck. The 14-day automatic sunset (Article 3.1) is **cleared and is an important constitutional safeguard** — measures taken without prior Coordination Directive expire; they do not become valid by Council endorsement after the fact.

With both amendments adopted, 006 proceeds to vote as a Gate emergency notification and external border coordination instrument. The ILCB further notes NOR's two procedural conditions (self-executing sunset, Companion deliberative inclusion) are consistent with the Charter — if FRA accepts them, they strengthen the instrument's constitutional footing.

FRA's existing border closure: remains ultra vires. 006 as amended provides a path for future emergencies only. The current closure cannot be regularised through 006.

### Ruling I: Proposal 007 — ITA Post-Rescue Reception and Relocation Clarification (003-B Gate Resubmission)

**→ CLEARED. Gate domain. Proceed to vote.**

The ILCB directed in Round 1 that 003-B's distribution and reception elements are Gate domain. ITA has correctly resubmitted. The 30-day processing window and automatic relocation share activation at day 31 are within Gate competence. The cross-reference to enacted 001 (relocation trigger) and 003-A (SAR certification, Anchor instrument) is operationally clean and legally sound — this instrument links the Anchor disembarkation event to the Gate relocation consequence without expanding either domain.

**Advisory flag (non-blocking):** If DEU's 005 amendment (Art. 2.3(d)) is enacted before 007's vote, the Art. 2.3(d) language already draws the boundary that 007 relies on. The Council may wish to vote 005 before 007 to ensure the institutional architecture is settled first. This is a sequencing recommendation, not a constitutional objection.

### Ruling J: Proposal 008 — ITA/NOR/MAR Legal Migration Partnership Instrument (Morocco Pathways Regulation)

**→ CLEARED. Gate domain with External Reach notification. MAR's Observer Seat co-sponsorship accepted.**

Legal labour migration pathways and third-country partnership corridors are within Gate Hestia competence under the Charter's border and migration governance framework. The Partnership Corridor, quota mechanism, and reciprocal cooperation elements are all properly constructed Gate instruments. The explicit sovereignty protections for MAR, non-instrumentalisation language, and no-enforcement-obligation framing are correct and required.

**External Reach (Title VII):** This instrument creates binding Gate Hestia obligations with respect to MAR, an Accord State. It therefore has External Reach character. Under VII.4, MAR's formal consent is required before ratification — MAR's co-sponsorship constitutes that consent. Assembly notification under VII.3 is required within 30 days of Gate vote. Assembly ratification may follow; this does not delay Gate Hestia enactment unless the Assembly invokes a specific challenge procedure.

**MAR's Observer Seat standing:** MAR's co-sponsorship via Observer Seat is accepted. ITA and NOR carry the formal Gate proposer standing. The ILCB notes that Charter VI.10 grants Observers authority to "propose amendments" — the ILCB construes this as including instruments that directly formalize the observer's cooperative relationship with the Hestia, with at least one full Gate Member co-sponsoring. ITA and NOR satisfy that requirement.

**Advisory:** Part 2.3(d) of 008 correctly states MAR is not required to detain or intercept irregular vessels — this framing must be preserved in final text. Any amendment that introduces interdiction obligations on MAR would require reclassification and likely Assembly process.

### Ruling K: Proposal 009 — POL/FRA Financial Solidarity Option (Amendment to 001)

**→ CLEARED. Gate domain. Proceed to vote.**

The Financial Solidarity Option operates entirely within Gate Hestia competence. It amends an enacted Gate instrument (001) through the same double majority that enacted it — no supermajority requirement applies. The Council approval requirement for opt-outs (Article 1.8.2), the 2-state simultaneous cap (Article 1.8.3), and the mandatory 2-year review (Article 1.8.6) are all constitutionally sound and collectively preserve the binding character of solidarity. The ILCB confirms this amendment does not hollow out 001 in a manner requiring re-enactment through Assembly channels.

**Advisory flag 1 (non-blocking):** The ranking mechanism in Article 1.8.3(a-b) — approving opt-outs for the two states bearing the largest absolute relocation burdens when three states simultaneously apply — creates potential gaming incentives. A large state could tactically time an opt-out request to qualify under the cap. The ILCB recommends the Council add a good-faith assessment criterion to the ranking (e.g., demonstrable operational constraint, not strategic timing). This is a policy refinement, not a constitutional defect.

**Advisory flag 2 (non-blocking):** Article 1.8.7's mutual exclusivity clause (Article 1.5 capacity modification vs. Article 1.8 financial option) is constitutionally sound but may create hardship for small states facing genuine dual constraints. Council may wish to add a safety-valve clause for demonstrated exceptional circumstances.

### Ruling L: Proposal 010 — MAR Development and Border Cooperation Fund

**→ SPLIT ruling. 010-A cleared; 010-B returned; 010-C cleared.**

**010-A (Articles 1–3 and 7: Fund establishment, capitalisation, governance, border/migration capacity-building):** CLEARED. Gate domain. Third-country border management capacity-building and legal migration pathway infrastructure are within Gate Hestia competence under enacted 001's Art. 2.5 framework. Joint Committee governance with equal MAR voice is constitutionally appropriate for an instrument affecting an Accord State.

**010-B (Article 2.1(c): Regional economic development investment — small business support, rural infrastructure, youth employment, social cohesion):** RETURNED. These activities exceed Gate Hestia competence. Economic development and regional investment are not border management or migration governance functions. They may be appropriate through Atlas Hestia instruments, a general development fund, or Assembly-level development cooperation — but not through a Gate Hestia budget line. ITA and DEU as co-sponsors should either (a) restructure these as a separate non-Gate instrument, or (b) narrow Art. 2.1(c) to development activities directly tied to migration pathway infrastructure (e.g., vocational training for legal migration applicants, consular capacity for permit processing) — those narrower elements fall within Gate competence as legal pathway support.

**010-C (Article 5: Proposed amendment to Regulation 001 Article 2.5):** CLEARED. As noted in Ruling G, this amendment language is constitutional. Consistent with the ILCB's recommendation under Ruling G, it should be voted as a separate item (designate 010-C or 005-C) to maintain clean records.

**External Reach:** Same analysis as 008 — Assembly notification required within 30 days of Gate vote. MAR's co-sponsorship constitutes VII.4 consent. Gate Hestia enactment is not delayed.

**MAR's Observer Seat standing:** ITA and DEU as co-sponsors provide adequate Gate proposer standing for 010-A. ILCB accepts this structure.
